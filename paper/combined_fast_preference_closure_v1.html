When Preferences Move Faster Than Equilibrium

Endogenous utility, induced Nash games, and selection in mathematical economics

June 24, 2026

JEL codes: C72, D01, D11, D83, D91, J13. Keywords: mathematical economics, game theory, endogenous preferences, fast utility, fertility, loneliness, recommender systems, AI companions, Nash equilibrium, material payoff, selection.

Abstract

Modern systems can make people sincerely want things that weaken the capacities they later need. A feed can train attention away from study or friendship. A betting app can make the next wager feel like participation rather than risk. An AI companion can reduce loneliness tonight while either rebuilding human social practice or replacing it. Once wants move this quickly, revealed preference is not only an input to economics. It is also an output of a preference-forming system.

The model has three parts. First, a preference-forming rule settles what a person wants in a given environment. Second, people optimize and play a Nash equilibrium in the induced subjective game. Third, consequences are evaluated by a separate material criterion such as health, fertility, solvency, social capacity, learning, or institutional continuity. The central object is a preference-capacity wedge: a locally sincere utility state that guides rational choice but weakens the slower capacity or institution being evaluated.

Three results follow. First, choices can be rational inside manufactured wants. The preferences are not fake; they are endogenous. Second, Nash equilibrium still describes strategic consistency after wants are formed, but it does not certify the preference-forming environment. Third, selection remains a survival method, but once different starting tastes are rapidly pushed into the same induced want, later behavior cannot tell those starting tastes apart. The durable selection question moves toward the platforms, institutions, peer networks, AI products, and social norms that repeatedly form tastes.

The cases fall into four families. Fertility and housing show moving thresholds: the standard for acceptable adulthood can rise faster than people can reach it. Loneliness, dating, and AI companions show capacity substitution: easier substitutes may either rebuild or replace the human skill they imitate. Gambling, debt, food, and appearance show reward capture: systems shorten the path to pleasure and delay the consequence. Politics and migration show civic reference points: anger, fear, or humiliation can persist after facts change. The evidence does not prove one grand cause. It identifies a repeated mechanism to test: fast preference formation, locally rational choice, a named evaluator, and a boundary condition.

The conditional predictions are sharp: family policy has limited force when the parenthood threshold is mainly social-status driven; AI companionship improves welfare when it increases real-world social capacity; fear can outlast crime or inflation shocks when the reference point itself has moved.

1. What Changes When Wants Move Fast

A rational person can optimize inside a manufactured want. A society can then accumulate rational choices that are costly for health, reproduction, learning, solvency, trust, or institutional continuity. The contradiction is only apparent: subjective utility and material persistence are different objects.

Utility What feels worth choosing after the environment has shaped desire.
Nash The consistency condition for choices in the induced subjective game.
Evaluator The application-specific criterion: health, fertility, solvency, skill, trust, or continuity.
Selection When initial tastes settle into the same state, the empirical survival question turns toward taste-shaping systems.
Policy The question is proxy alignment: what is the system optimizing while forming wants?

Economics often treats preferences as given because the simplification is powerful. If tastes change slowly, the analyst can hold utility fixed and study prices, constraints, games, contracts, and institutions. That is still the right move for many questions. The move fails when the object called "preference" changes on the same time scale as the choice, or faster.

That faster world is no longer exotic. A feed can test thousands of tiny variations and learn which ones keep a person watching. A dating app can change the perceived cost of romantic effort. A sports-betting app can turn a game into a sequence of micro-risks. A beauty market can convert appearance into a continuously scored asset. A political influencer can become more trusted than an institution because the influencer feels familiar. An AI companion can respond with infinite patience and no ordinary social friction. These systems do not merely reveal demand. They help create the demand later revealed.

Result in one sentence. When preference formation is fast, the economist must distinguish the utility people maximize from the process that formed that utility and from the slower criterion used to evaluate the consequences.

Equilibrium applies after wants are formed. Nash equilibrium is a mathematical consistency concept, not a moral endorsement. Once the preference-forming process has settled, play may reach a Nash equilibrium in the induced game. The result can be perfectly coherent as game theory and still poor as demography, public health, human capital, household finance, or institutional continuity.

Selection operates on what persists and reproduces. The fast limit changes the plausible object of selection. If many initial taste states are quickly pushed into the same induced utility state, later behavior cannot tell those starting states apart. Empirically, the natural object to study is then the environment that repeatedly forms tastes: families, schools, religious communities, neighborhoods, apps, platforms, AI systems, and legal rules.

The central wedge is therefore not between "real" and "fake" preferences. The preferences are real in the ordinary behavioral sense: people can sincerely act on them. The wedge is between locally compelling wants and a slower evaluator. The evaluator must always be named: fertility, health, solvency, learning, social capacity, and democratic legitimacy are not the same criterion.

2. Ten Live Puzzles

Each case below has its own literature and its own causes. The diagnostic is simple: identify the fast preference-forming loop, identify the local choice it makes attractive, and then ask whether the induced wants strengthen or weaken a slower evaluator.

Four families keep recurring across otherwise different problems. Fertility and housing are moving-threshold problems: the standard for acceptable adulthood moves faster than many people can reach it. Loneliness, dating, and AI companionship are capacity-substitution problems: easier substitutes can either rebuild or replace the human skill they imitate. Gambling, debt, food, and appearance are reward-capture problems: systems shorten the path to pleasure and delay the consequence, while GLP-1s show that fast preference change can also repair. Outrage, crime fear, economic mood, migration, and alliances are civic reference-point problems: the salient story can persist after conditions change.

The same diagnostic handles harms and repairs because it asks what capacity the system leaves stronger or weaker, what proxy selects that change, and which slower capacity is being measured.

3. Fertility, Housing, And The Moving Target Of Adulthood

Fertility is often discussed as if the preference for children either exists or disappears. That misses the middle layer. Many people may still value children, but the preference environment can change the conditions under which having children feels responsible, attractive, or socially admissible.

The OECD fertility review documents a long decline in fertility across rich countries and ties it to labor markets, gender norms, housing, uncertainty, family policy, and personal conditions. UNFPA's 2025 report shifts the emphasis from panic about low births to reproductive agency: many people report that cost of living, insecure futures, unequal care burdens, partner constraints, and health factors prevent them from having the number of children they want. Eurostat reports an EU total fertility rate of 1.34 in 2024. The United States remains below replacement as well, with CDC/NCHS reporting low recent fertility and births.

Fast preference formation enters through the idea of an "adequate" adult life. If visible comparison raises the standard for housing, schools, extracurricular investment, career stability, body image, travel, and parental attention, then children become subjectively expensive even when income has risen relative to earlier generations. In the model, the cost is not only a budget constraint. It is a utility state: a person may sincerely prefer delay because the induced taste system says that parenting before a high threshold is irresponsible or humiliating.

Housing sharpens the point. The Harvard Joint Center for Housing Studies documents severe affordability pressures and cost burdens. The National Association of Realtors reported a historically low first-time-buyer share and older first-time buyers in its recent buyer profile. Housing affects fertility in the familiar budgetary way, but the fast-preference lens adds a positional layer: the social meaning of a "good enough" home can move faster than the physical stock of housing. A household may be financially able to have a child by older standards while feeling unable by the current status standard.

Demographic mechanism. Low fertility can be modeled as a failure of realized agency rather than as a simple disappearance of family desire: fast comparison raises the subjective threshold for acceptable parenthood, and the induced utility can rationally select postponement or smaller families.

The prediction is concrete but conditional. If the binding preference-forming rule is a status race over adequate adulthood, then pronatalist cash transfers alone should have limited effect. Stronger effects should come from changes that lower the socially induced threshold: reliable childcare, housing supply in family-forming places, predictable work schedules, reduced education-status arms races, visible normalization of good-enough parenting, and partner-market conditions that make commitment less risky. The right empirical test is not merely whether people say they want children. It is whether the environment makes the wanted child feel feasible without heroic status performance.

4. Loneliness, Dating, And Synthetic Ease

Loneliness is not simply the absence of contact. It is often the absence of reliable, reciprocal, embodied connection. That distinction matters because digital and AI systems can supply contact-like relief while leaving the harder capacity untouched.

The WHO Commission on Social Connection treats loneliness as a global health issue, and the US Surgeon General's advisory describes social disconnection as a serious risk to health and community. Yet modern life offers more channels for contact than any prior period. The paradox is easier to understand once preferences are endogenous. If entertainment, mediated contact, asynchronous interaction, and low-risk parasocial connection reduce the immediate cost of solitude, a person can rationally choose the substitute today. If that substitute reduces practice at initiating, repairing, and sustaining ordinary relationships, tomorrow's human interaction becomes more costly. Avoidance then becomes more attractive because it has already weakened the capacity required for return.

Dating and sex fit the same pattern without being identical. Ueda et al. (2020) document increased sexual inactivity among US adults aged 18 to 44 from 2000 to 2018, with especially large changes among younger men. CDC youth surveys also show long-run changes in adolescent sexual behavior. The model does not require a single villain. Apps, pornography, games, safety norms, reputational fear, economic insecurity, and online comparison can all raise the perceived cost of courtship. If lower effort then reduces confidence, social networks, and opportunity, the fast utility of avoidance becomes self-confirming.

AI companions intensify the mechanism because they can offer the reward of social response without ordinary reciprocity. Common Sense Media reports substantial teen use of AI companions. De Freitas et al. find that AI companions can reduce loneliness in experimental and longitudinal settings. Both facts can be true. Acute loneliness relief is valuable. The long-run question is whether the companion is a bridge or a sink.

AI companion design as preference formation.

Design Immediate want it strengthens Long-run evaluator Observable success

Social-capacity result. The same technology can reduce or deepen a preference-capacity wedge. It helps when it makes the difficult human capacity easier to practice. It harms when it makes the substitute so satisfying that the capacity decays.

5. Reward Loops: Gambling, Debt, Food, And Appearance

Some wedges look less like ideology and more like trained reward. These are systems that shorten pleasure and lengthen consequence. The old model of temptation is still relevant, but the speed and personalization have changed. A platform can learn the exact timing, graphic, social cue, and offer that moves a person from weak interest to repeated use.

Frictionless Risk And Purchase

Online sports betting is a clean case because it turns fandom into a continuous market for micro-risk. NBER research links online sports betting legalization and access to financial stress, including lower savings and worse credit outcomes for some households. The American Gaming Association tracks the growth of commercial gaming revenue, and sports betting has become a large, routine consumer market. The fast-formation mechanism is not merely "people like risk." It is that the app can make the next small wager feel like participation, identity, and attention at once.

Buy-now-pay-later and identity consumption have a related structure. Federal Reserve household well-being data report BNPL use and financial strain among users. A purchase can close a gap between the current self and the visible aspirational self. The debt consequence arrives later, diffusely, and often privately. The utility correction is immediate, vivid, and often social.

Repair And Arms Race

Food shows why the model should not become anti-pleasure moralizing. The CDC documents high ultra-processed-food calorie shares. Food environments can train cravings that work against metabolic health. GLP-1 drugs are a candidate repair mechanism: clinical evidence such as Wilding et al. shows large weight effects, while clinical and consumer discussions suggest changes in appetite and craving. The point is not that medicine should replace judgment. The formal point is that endogenous utility can be harmful or beneficial depending on the evaluator and selection proxy. A preference-forming system is a bridge when it leaves the person more capable outside the system; it is a sink when it becomes the place where the capacity is consumed.

Teen attention and identity loops make the mechanism visible earlier in life. Pew's teen technology surveys document near-constant social media use among many adolescents, and Pew's teen mental-health survey reports that many teens see social media as having a negative effect on people their age. The model's warning is not that online identity communities are bad; for some youth they are protective. The warning is that a temporary feeling can become a rewarded label, then a searchable community, then a self-preserving preference state.

Appearance markets, including lookmaxxing and early cosmetic normalization, add a positional layer. The American Society of Plastic Surgeons documents a large cosmetic-procedure market. A platformed face is not simply a face; it is a ranked, filtered, comparable asset. If each person acts rationally inside a beauty arms race, average anxiety and expenditure can rise even if no one intended the race. Social feedback can move the branch: yesterday's enhanced look becomes today's baseline, and the old baseline begins to feel inadequate.

Reward-loop implication. Fast utility can make the next bet, purchase, snack, procedure, or scroll rational at the moment of choice while weakening financial resilience, metabolic health, attention, or body satisfaction over the slower horizon.

6. Politics And International Spillovers

Politics is full of preferences that are locally rewarding and institutionally costly. The preference for outrage can be enjoyable because it supplies clarity, group belonging, moral rank, and enemy identification. The institution damaged by that preference is not an individual's private welfare alone. It is deliberation, legitimacy, peaceful competition, and the public's ability to coordinate on shared facts.

Pew's news-influencer work shows that a substantial share of US adults, especially younger adults, regularly get news from influencers. The problem is not that influencers are always wrong. Many are useful translators. The preference-formation risk is that trust migrates from institutions and correction processes toward personalities who feel familiar. Research on TikTok engagement during the 2024 US election and platform-ranking audits suggest that engagement systems can reward divisive or toxic content more than what users say they reflectively want. Gallup and Pew document low institutional trust. In the model, distrust can become self-reinforcing: distrust makes correction less rewarding, and lack of correction confirms distrust.

Crime fear and economic mood show why this is not only about ideology. Pew's work on local news and crime perception shows how violent crime can become highly salient through exposure, while FBI preliminary data report recent national declines in violent crime. National declines do not refute local danger or underreporting; they show why the level of fear and the level of measured crime must be analyzed separately. Inflation memory has a similar structure: even after some macro indicators improve, Brookings and public-opinion work describe household sentiment as shaped by price memories, housing costs, insurance, groceries, and partisan attribution. These are not imaginary grievances. The preference-formation question is how a recent shock becomes a durable reference point for trust and political choice.

Migration shows the international version. The OECD reports high migration flows, while UNHCR reports historically high forced displacement. Aging economies may need workers and caregivers, but publics may experience migration through housing pressure, border imagery, crime stories, language anxiety, or national-identity threat. Pew found that most Americans viewed the US-Mexico border situation as a crisis or major problem in early 2024; Gallup later found that the desire to reduce immigration fell sharply in 2025. The swing is consistent with salience-sensitive preferences, while also reflecting real border conditions, policy changes, media agenda, and partisan cueing.

The global implication is sharp. If a country's political media reward unilateralism, contempt for allies, or suspicion of international institutions, domestic utility can move in a direction that weakens alliance coordination, migration integration, sanctions support, or climate cooperation. Pew's 2026 global survey reports declining ratings for the United States and greater concern about its reliability among publics in many countries; those publics may become more receptive to hedging even when shared security or trade interests remain. Conversely, a preference-forming rule that makes competence, reciprocity, and credible administration rewarding can strengthen institutional capacity.

Political implication. Political preferences can become reference-point preferences: citizens choose the information environment that preserves anger, fear, or humiliation even when that weakens the institutions they later need.

7. Equilibrium After Wants Are Formed

A betting app example shows the separation. The user may maximize the utility of the next in-game wager; the platform may select exposures that increase betting; the material evaluator may be household liquidity. Those three objects can point in different directions.

The formal core is the discipline of not collapsing those objects. The first object is the preference-forming rule, whose limiting map records where fast preference adjustment settles. The second object is the subjective game: after wants form, each player maximizes the utility that now feels like the relevant payoff. The third is the material evaluator: the criterion used to judge the resulting outcome.

The fast-limit assumption says that the preference state settles before the slower economic or institutional process changes much. This is an extreme case, but useful extremes reveal what is invariant. In that limit, equilibrium analysis becomes ordinary game theory applied to the induced subjective game. The math does not say that Nash equilibrium is obsolete. It says that Nash equilibrium begins after preference formation has already done important work.

Existing economics already contains many pieces of this story. Stigler and Becker defend stable preferences by moving variation into shadow prices; Becker and Murphy formalize habit and addiction; Bowles, Bisin and Verdier, and Bernheim et al. study endogenous, transmitted, or chosen preferences.

Dekel, Ely, and Yilankaya ask which preferences survive selection. Recommender-system work studies preference-shaping, feedback loops, and engagement incentives (Adomavicius et al.; Jiang et al.; Chaney, Stewart, and Engelhardt; Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan). The fast limit separates the timing: first the environment settles wants, then players optimize, then the result is evaluated by a named material criterion.

The strongest invariance condition is best-response preservation. If fast preference formation leaves every player's best response unchanged at every opponent strategy, then the subjective and material games have the same Nash predictions. That is the clean case. It is also demanding. When preference formation changes best responses, Nash predictions can change. Whether the change is materially good or bad depends on the evaluator.

The selection theorem explains why the Darwinian question can move up one level. If different initial tastes are all rapidly mapped into the same settled taste state, then a selection rule that observes only later play cannot distinguish those initial tastes. That theorem does not by itself prove a full evolutionary dynamic over institutions. It identifies the next empirical object: the environments and rules that keep producing preferences. In ordinary language, when the app, institution, school, peer group, or AI system rewrites tastes quickly enough, the survival question increasingly concerns the preference-forming system.

Social feedback gives the mechanism a sharp threshold. Below the threshold, exposure is reversible. Above it, a temporary shock can move the system to a new stable branch. That is the mathematical version of a meme, norm, body standard, political identity, or parenting ideal that no longer needs the original shock to persist.

8. Tests And Policy

The model becomes empirical when the evaluator is explicit. Fertility examples require measures of desired and realized children, housing thresholds, partner-market friction, parenting norms, and cohort timing. Loneliness examples require separating contact from capacity: whether digital or AI interaction increases later human interaction, reciprocal support, and health. Gambling and debt examples require linking exposure, app design, betting frequency, liquidity, and credit outcomes. Political examples require measuring the gap between engagement, reflected preference, factual accuracy, trust, and institutional behavior.

Diagnostics suggested by the model.

Diagnostic Empirical question Useful evidence Interpretation
Speed How quickly do values, habits, search, or attention change after exposure? Rollouts, outages, randomized ranking changes, cohort timing, high-frequency surveys. Fast movement makes preference formation part of the economic object.
Reversibility Do wants return when exposure ends, or does a new branch persist? Longitudinal follow-up after product exits, bans, shocks, or algorithm changes. Persistent branch changes require stronger interventions than reversible nudges.
Proxy alignment What does the system optimize while forming preferences? Engagement, retention, satisfaction, reflected goals, health, learning, savings, social ties. Preference change is harmful only relative to a criterion.
Capacity Does the preference-forming rule build or erode the ability to act outside the system? Offline relationships, classroom persistence, savings buffers, social skill, work output. Many material evaluators depend on capacities, not only current choices.
Selection level Are initial tastes or taste-forming systems doing the real work? Comparisons across schools, platforms, families, legal regimes, recommendation objectives, AI designs. In the fast limit, durable differences are especially likely to appear at the level of institutions and preference-forming rules.

Policy follows the same separation. If the problem is a budget constraint, subsidize or deregulate the constrained object. If the problem is reversible influence, disclosure, friction, and choice architecture may help. If the problem is persistent branch selection, small nudges may fail because the induced taste state is self-reinforcing. If the problem is proxy misalignment, the central policy lever is the objective function of the system forming preferences.

AI makes that policy question more precise. A companion or assistant should not be evaluated only by engagement or user affection. It should be evaluated by whether the user becomes more capable outside the system. A recommender should not be evaluated only by watch time. It should be evaluated by reflected satisfaction, sleep, learning, social capacity, and the user's ability to leave without losing function. A political platform should not be evaluated only by expression and activity. It should be evaluated by whether it preserves the institutional preconditions of peaceful disagreement.

Limits. The model does not imply that low fertility is immoral, that online life is bad, that migrants are merely economic inputs, that AI companions are inherently harmful, or that institutional distrust is never earned. Its claim is narrower and more useful: when preferences are formed quickly by systems with measurable objectives, revealed choice alone cannot settle the welfare or survival question.

9. Conclusion

Modern preference technologies make an old assumption fragile. If wants are slow, utility can be treated as a primitive. If wants are fast, utility becomes a state variable produced by institutions, platforms, peer networks, and AI systems. People then optimize inside that state, and their choices can be rational without being aligned with the slower capacities that keep lives and societies working.

The useful conclusion is not pessimistic. The same mechanism that can produce dependency, isolation, debt, status anxiety, political outrage, or fertility blockage can also be used for repair. A preference-forming system can make exercise easier, conversation less frightening, learning more rewarding, appetite less compulsive, parenting more feasible, and disagreement less humiliating. The difference is the proxy. Engagement design treats the next session as success. Scaffolding design treats the user's expanded life outside the system as success.

Nash equilibrium still disciplines strategic claims: it describes consistency among choices after wants form. Darwinian selection still disciplines survival claims: it can be applied to the systems that keep reproducing taste-forming environments and their effects. In fast-preference economies, the central object is the preference-forming rule, the system that makes some wants feel natural before anyone starts optimizing.

Appendix A. Formal Model And General Results

A.1 Primitives

There is a finite set of players \(N=\{1,\ldots,n\}\). Player \(i\) has a finite action set \(A_i\), and \(A=\prod_i A_i\). The slow economic environment is \(E\). A preference-forming rule is denoted by \(\ell\). In this appendix only, a closure law means the limiting rule that records where fast preference adjustment settles; it is not a special game-theory term. Player \(i\)'s subjective utility is \(U_i(a;\theta_i,E,\ell)\), where \(\theta_i\) is the preference state used for choice. The material payoff or evaluator is \(\pi_i(a;E,\ell)\). The two payoff objects may coincide, but the model does not assume they do.

Subjective And Material Payoffs \[ U_i(a;\theta_i,E,\ell), \qquad \pi_i(a;E,\ell). \]

The preference state follows a fast dynamic. The dot denotes a derivative with respect to ordinary time, \(T_\theta>0\) is the preference-adjustment timescale, \(H\) is the fast vector field, and \(\zeta\) is a fast exposure or transition input.

Fast Preference Dynamics \[ T_\theta \dot{\theta}=H(\theta;E,\ell,\zeta). \]

During the fast adjustment, \(E\) and \(\ell\) are held fixed; \(\zeta\) is either fixed or specified as the fast input path. Let \(q\) be the initial preference state. The closure map \(C_\ell\) is the selected post-adjustment preference state.

Settling Map And Fixed-Input Unique Branch \[ \begin{aligned} C_\ell(E,q,\zeta) &= \lim_{s\to\infty}\theta(s;q,E,\ell,\zeta),\\ \theta_{\ell,\zeta}^\star(E) &= C_\ell(E,q,\zeta) \quad \text{for all relevant }q \quad \text{after fixing }(E,\ell,\zeta). \end{aligned} \]

Write \(C_{i,\ell}(E,q,\zeta)\) for player \(i\)'s component of \(C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)\). The reduced subjective game is the finite game obtained after substituting the settled preference state into subjective utility.

Reduced Subjective Game \[ \Gamma_{\ell,q,\zeta}^\star(E) = \left(N,(A_i)_{i\in N}, \left(U_i(\cdot;C_{i,\ell}(E,q,\zeta),E,\ell)\right)_{i\in N}\right). \]

In the fixed-input unique-settling benchmark, \(\Gamma_{\ell,\zeta}^\star(E)\) is shorthand for the same reduced subjective game with \(C_{i,\ell}(E,q,\zeta)=\theta_{i,\ell,\zeta}^\star(E)\) for every player and every relevant initial state.

Fast Preference Settling With The Slow Environment Held Constant

Fix \(E\), \(\ell\), and the relevant input \(\zeta\). In the fixed-input unique-settling benchmark, suppose the fast preference dynamic has a unique globally attracting fixed point \(\theta_{\ell,\zeta}^\star(E)\) on the relevant input class. Then, after the fast preference adjustment and in the limit \(T_\theta\to0\), the limiting payoff table at \((E,\ell,\zeta)\) is represented by the reduced subjective game \(\Gamma_{\ell,\zeta}^\star(E)\). More generally, if the fast limit from initial state \(q\) exists and equals \(C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)\), the limiting payoff table for that settling experiment is represented by \(\Gamma_{\ell,q,\zeta}^\star(E)\).

Proof. On the fast time \(s=t/T_\theta\), the preference subsystem is \(d\theta/ds=H(\theta;E,\ell,\zeta)\). Under fixed-input unique settling, every initial state in the relevant basin converges to \(\theta_{\ell,\zeta}^\star(E)\). Thus the payoff table used for play converges pointwise to \(U_i(\cdot;\theta_{i,\ell,\zeta}^\star(E),E,\ell)\) for every player. Because action sets are finite, the limiting finite game is well defined. The branch-selected case is identical with \(C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)\) replacing \(\theta_{\ell,\zeta}^\star(E)\). The lemma identifies the limiting game; it does not assert convergence of equilibrium selections without an additional selection rule.

For compact notation, this subsection fixes the input \(\zeta\) and writes the unique-settling case using \(\theta_{i,\ell,\zeta}^\star(E)\). In a branch-selected experiment, replace \(\theta_{i,\ell,\zeta}^\star(E)\) everywhere by the realized settled component \(C_{i,\ell}(E,q,\zeta)\). The best-response argument is unchanged once the realized reduced game is fixed.

Expected Payoffs \[ \begin{aligned} \bar U_{i,\ell,\zeta}(x_i,x_{-i};E) &= \sum_{a\in A}x(a)\, U_i(a;\theta_{i,\ell,\zeta}^\star(E),E,\ell),\\ \bar\pi_{i,\ell}(x_i,x_{-i};E) &= \sum_{a\in A}x(a)\,\pi_i(a;E,\ell). \end{aligned} \]

The material benchmark game uses the same players and actions, but evaluates play by \(\pi_i\) rather than settled subjective utility. For any finite game \(\Gamma\), let \(NE(\Gamma)\) denote its mixed Nash equilibrium set.

Material Benchmark Game \[ \Gamma_\ell^\pi(E) = \left(N,(A_i)_{i\in N}, \left(\pi_i(\cdot;E,\ell)\right)_{i\in N}\right). \]

Define subjective and material best-response correspondences by maximizing these expected payoffs over \(X_i\).

Best-Response Correspondences \[ \begin{aligned} BR_{i,\ell,\zeta}^\star(x_{-i};E) &= \arg\max_{x_i\in X_i} \bar U_{i,\ell,\zeta}(x_i,x_{-i};E),\\ BR_{i,\ell}^\pi(x_{-i};E) &= \arg\max_{x_i\in X_i} \bar\pi_{i,\ell}(x_i,x_{-i};E). \end{aligned} \]
Mixed Best-Response Invariance

If \(BR_{i,\ell,\zeta}^\star(x_{-i};E)=BR_{i,\ell}^\pi(x_{-i};E)\) for every player \(i\) and every mixed opponent profile \(x_{-i}\in X_{-i}\), then the subjective reduced game and the material benchmark game have the same mixed Nash equilibrium set. For a fixed game, the condition is sufficient but not necessary.

Proof. A mixed Nash equilibrium is exactly a fixed point of the players' best-response correspondences. If every player's subjective and material best-response correspondences are equal at every opponent mixed profile, the two fixed-point sets are identical.

Strategically Irrelevant Preference Formation

Suppose that for every player \(i\) there exist \(\beta_i>0\) and \(\alpha_i(a_{-i};E,\ell)\), independent of \(a_i\), such that

Opponent-Only Transformation \[ U_i(a_i,a_{-i};\theta_{i,\ell,\zeta}^\star(E),E,\ell) = \alpha_i(a_{-i};E,\ell) + \beta_i\pi_i(a_i,a_{-i};E,\ell). \]

Then the subjective and material mixed Nash sets coincide.

Proof. For fixed \(x_{-i}\), the expected \(\alpha_i\) term is constant in \(x_i\). Multiplication by \(\beta_i>0\) preserves the ordering of all own mixed strategies. Therefore subjective and material best responses coincide everywhere, and Proposition 1 applies.

Best-Response Non-Invariance Test

If for some player \(i\), actions \(a_i,b_i\), and opponent mixed profile \(x_{-i}\), action \(a_i\) is the unique material best response while \(b_i\ne a_i\) is the unique subjective best response, then mixed best-response invariance fails. Nash sets may still coincide in special games, but the strong invariance test fails.

Proof. At the same opponent mixed profile, the two best-response correspondences are distinct singleton sets.

A Two-Action Nash-Set Change

Let two players each choose \(A\) or \(B\). For both players and every opponent action, material payoff satisfies \(\pi_i(A,a_{-i})=1\) and \(\pi_i(B,a_{-i})=0\). Settled subjective payoff reverses the ranking: \(U_i(A,a_{-i})=0\) and \(U_i(B,a_{-i})=1\). Then \((A,A)\) is the unique material Nash equilibrium and \((B,B)\) is the unique subjective Nash equilibrium.

A.3 Selection And Proxy Choice

Two initial preference states are settling-equivalent at \((E,\ell,\zeta)\) if they induce the same settled preference state.

Settling Equivalence \[ q\sim_{E,\ell,\zeta}q' \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)=C_\ell(E,q',\zeta). \]

An admissible post-adjustment selection rule observes an initial preference state only through the reduced subjective game, the selected Nash set, the material evaluator, and the slow environment. Formally, write:

Post-Adjustment Selection Rule \[ S(q) = \Phi\!\left( NE(\Gamma^\star_{\ell,q,\zeta}(E)), \pi(\cdot;E,\ell), E,\ell \right). \]
Selection Target Shift

Fix \(E\), \(\ell\), and \(\zeta\). If \(q\sim_{E,\ell,\zeta}q'\), then the two initial states induce the same reduced subjective game and the same Nash set. Consequently, every admissible post-adjustment selection rule \(S\) is constant on each settling-equivalence class. Under common unique settling, selection over initial preference states is degenerate in the \(T_\theta\to0\) limit.

Proof. Settling equivalence gives the same settled preference state, hence the same subjective payoff table and reduced game. Any post-adjustment admissible selection rule receives the same argument for \(q\) and \(q'\), so it gives the same value.

For institutional choice, let a rule \(r\in\mathcal R\) induce a settled preference state, a reduced subjective game, selected play, a platform proxy \(V(r)\), and a material evaluator \(G(r)\). A platform chooses \(r^V\in\arg\max_{\mathcal R}V(r)\), while a material planner would choose \(r^G\in\arg\max_{\mathcal R}G(r)\).

Proxy Alignment

For a fixed feasible set \(\mathcal R\), if \(\arg\max_{r\in\mathcal R}V(r)\subseteq\arg\max_{r\in\mathcal R}G(r)\), then every proxy-maximizing rule attains the feasible maximum of the material evaluator. Conversely, if there exist \(r_1,r_2\in\mathcal R\) with \(V(r_1)>V(r_2)\) and \(G(r_1)<G(r_2)\), then on the feasible set \(\{r_1,r_2\}\), proxy maximization selects a materially inferior preference-forming rule.

Proof. The first claim follows directly from the inclusion of maximizer sets. The converse is the two-rule feasible set construction.

Appendix B. Social-Feedback Preference Formation

There are \(n\) agents with settled taste intensities \(m_i\in[-1,1]\). The baseline field is \(h_i\), the nonnegative attention matrix is \(W\), social reinforcement is \(\eta\), responsiveness is \(\beta\), a temporary exposure field is \(z\), and exposure loading is \(\phi_i\). The fast social-feedback preference-formation law is:

Network Social-Feedback Dynamics \[ T_m\dot m_i = -m_i + \tanh\!\left(\beta\left[h_i+\eta\sum_{j=1}^n W_{ij}m_j+\phi_i z\right]\right). \]
Subcritical Social-Feedback Dynamics

If \(\beta\eta\rho(W)<1\), where \(\rho(W)\) is the spectral radius of \(W\), then the network social-feedback dynamic has a unique globally attracting settled taste state. Small changes in \(z\) move that state continuously. When \(z\) is removed, the preference-forming rule itself has no hysteresis.

Proof. The right-hand side fixed-point map \(F(m,z)\) is a contraction in a weighted sup norm under the spectral-radius condition; write its Lipschitz constant in that norm as \(L<1\). Banach's fixed-point theorem gives a unique fixed point and continuous dependence on \(z\). For the ODE \(\dot m=(F(m,z)-m)/T_m\), the same contraction gives exponential decay of the distance between any trajectory and the fixed point at rate at least \((1-L)/T_m\) in the chosen norm. Hence the fixed point is globally attracting. Hysteresis requires multiple stable branches, which contraction excludes.

At a stable fixed point \(m^\star\), define \(D=\operatorname{diag}(1-(m_i^\star)^2)\). Differentiating the fixed-point equation with respect to the exposure field gives the local multiplier:

Local Influence Multiplier \[ \frac{\partial m^\star}{\partial z} = \left(I-\beta\eta DW\right)^{-1}\beta D\phi. \]
Near-Critical Influence Multiplier

If \(I-\beta\eta DW\) is invertible, the local derivative of settled preferences with respect to a small exposure field is given by the multiplier above. As \(\beta\eta\rho(DW)\) approaches one from below, the derivative can become large along the critical Perron or singular direction when \(D\phi\) has nonzero projection on that direction. This is a local derivative statement, not a finite-shock prediction.

Proof. Differentiate \(m=F(m,z)\) with respect to \(z\) and rearrange. The inverse becomes large as the relevant eigenvalue of \(\beta\eta DW\) approaches one from below.

The mean-field case sets all agents equal. Let \(m\) be average taste, \(J>0\) average reinforcement, \(\beta>0\) responsiveness, and \(b=h+z\) the total field.

Mean-Field Social-Feedback Equation \[ m=\tanh\!\left(\beta(Jm+b)\right). \]

If \(\beta J>1\), define the fold, or spinodal, taste level \(m_s\) and critical field \(B_c\) by:

Critical Exposure Field \[ m_s=\sqrt{1-\frac{1}{\beta J}}, \qquad B_c=Jm_s-\frac{\operatorname{arctanh}(m_s)}{\beta}. \]

The positive fold \(+B_c\) that destroys the low branch occurs at \(m=-m_s\). The negative fold \(-B_c\) that destroys the high branch occurs symmetrically at \(m=+m_s\). This sign convention is useful because a positive exposure shock must push the total field above \(+B_c\) to eliminate the low branch.

Hysteresis And Persistent Fashion

If \(\beta J>1\), then for every total field \(b\) with \(|b|<B_c\), the mean-field dynamic has two stable taste states separated by an unstable state. Starting on the low branch, a temporary positive shock that pushes \(b\) strictly above \(B_c\) eliminates that branch. If the shock is held long enough on the fast time scale for the state to enter the high branch's post-shock basin, and if the shock is then removed while the baseline field lies inside \((-B_c,B_c)\), the path remains on the high branch.

Proof. Folds occur where the mean-field fixed-point curve is tangent to the forty-five-degree line. The tangency condition gives \(\beta J(1-m_s^2)=1\), and substitution gives \(B_c\). A shock past the fold removes the low branch. If the state evolves long enough under the shocked field to enter the basin that maps to the high branch after removal, the high branch remains stable inside the hysteresis window.

Using the simple material evaluator \(G(m)=hm\), persistent material loss is possible when the baseline field favors the low branch and a temporary shock can cross the upper fold.

Persistent-Loss Window \[ |h|<B_c, \qquad B_c-h<z_{\max}. \]
Temporary Exposure, Persistent Material Loss

If the persistent-loss window holds, \(h<0\), and the initial condition is on the low stable branch, then any temporary positive exposure shock \(z\in(B_c-h,z_{\max})\) can move the system to the high stable branch when held long enough on the fast time scale. After the shock disappears, the new taste state persists along that path and lowers \(G\).

Proof. The inequalities put the baseline inside the hysteresis window and allow a shock to cross the upper critical field. The fast-time holding condition gives the basin transition described by the hysteresis proposition. When \(h<0\), the material evaluator ranks lower \(m\) above higher \(m\). The shock path ends on the high branch, so it lowers \(G\) relative to the initial low branch.
