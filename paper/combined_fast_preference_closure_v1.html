Fast Preference Closure And Social Feedback

Endogenous utility, Nash equilibrium, selection, and fashion-driven preference formation

June 23, 2026. Combined working draft.

JEL codes: C72, D01, D11, D83, D91. Keywords: endogenous preferences, Nash equilibrium, social interactions, fashion, memes, recommender systems, material payoff, selection.

Abstract

This paper studies finite games in which the utility used for choice is not fixed in advance. A fast preference-formation process first produces the utility representation that players use. Players then choose a Nash equilibrium in that induced subjective game. Material payoff is kept separate and is used afterward to evaluate survival, welfare, or institutional success. The model therefore has two payoff tables: one that players maximize and one that the analyst or selection process uses to judge consequences.

The motivating mechanism is social feedback. Fashions, influencers, memes, bubbles, and recommender systems are modeled as changing what people come to want by changing what is visible and socially reinforced. The paper treats these forces as possible closure laws, not as loose psychology. A worked social-feedback closure shows a sharp threshold: below criticality, exposure has a unique reversible effect; above criticality, temporary exposure can select a persistent preference branch.

Formally, \(E\) denotes the slow economic environment and \(\ell\) denotes the preference-formation rule, or closure law. During the fast adjustment, \(E\) and \(\ell\) are held constant while the preference state \(\theta\) moves. The closure map is \(C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)\), where \(q\) is the initial preference state and \(\zeta\) is any fast exposure or transition input. In the unique-closure case this collapses to \(\theta_\ell^\star(E)\), which produces a reduced subjective game \(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E)\). The first general result is a reduction: after the fast adjustment, equilibrium analysis is analysis of the reduced game. The second gives a strong sufficient condition under which the subjective and material games have the same Nash set, meaning the same collection of self-consistent mixed-strategy outcomes. The condition is plain: for every possible mixed behavior of the other players, each player must have the same set of optimal responses under subjective utility and material payoff.

The third general result concerns selection. If many initial preference states all close to the same final utility representation, then a material selection rule that observes only post-closure play cannot distinguish those initial states. The remaining selection problem is over what still differs after closure: laws, institutions, platform rules, or environments. A platform application makes the material-evaluation issue concrete. Preference change by itself is not a material loss. Loss follows when the proxy that chooses the preference-producing rule is misaligned with the material evaluator.

The social-feedback closure gives the abstract theory a concrete mechanism. In the mean-field case \(m=\tanh(\beta(Jm+b))\), if \(\beta J>1\), a temporary fashion or meme shock that crosses a critical field can move tastes from one stable branch to another. In a calibrated example, the positive shock needed is \(0.2135\), and a shock of \(0.35\) selects the high stable branch in the model. Numerical audits then separate two facts: exact Nash invariance is restrictive in random games, and social-feedback closure can be reversible or history-dependent depending on criticality.

1. Introduction

In a standard finite game, each player chooses an action from a finite list. The model gives each player a payoff table, and the players choose strategies. A Nash equilibrium is a self-consistent outcome: given what everyone else is doing, no player wants to change. A mixed strategy is a probability distribution over actions; a Nash set is the collection of all self-consistent mixed profiles. In the standard formulation, the payoff table is part of the primitives of the model.

This paper changes one part of that setup. The payoff table used for choice is allowed to be produced by a preference-formation process. The motivating cases are familiar: habit, socialization, identity, advertising, cultural transmission, recommender systems, and AI-mediated environments can all affect what a person comes to value. The limiting question is deliberately sharp: what if that preference-producing process is much faster than the other economic state variables?

The concrete mechanism studied later is social feedback. Fashions, influencers, memes, and online bubbles can be represented as exposure fields that alter tastes while social reinforcement makes those altered tastes appear more common. This is not a new theory of fads by itself; bandwagon demand, social interactions, threshold contagion, and influence diffusion are established literatures. The move here is to use those mechanisms as fast utility-closure laws and then ask which game is played after closure.

The sequence is direct. Preferences settle first. Players then optimize using the settled subjective utility representation. The analyst then evaluates the material consequences of the resulting play. This order is the paper's main object.

The model separates subjective utility from material payoff. Subjective utility is the representation players maximize. Material payoff is the criterion used for survival, growth, welfare accounting, or external evaluation. A closure law is the rule or dynamic that says where the fast preference state settles, given the slow environment. Once that state settles, it induces the subjective game in which players choose strategies. Material payoff is evaluated only after that induced game is solved.

The contribution is not the claim that preferences are endogenous. That claim is familiar from habit formation, addiction, cultural transmission, advertising, identity, and indirect evolutionary preferences. The contribution is the timing in the fast-preference limit. In indirect evolutionary preference models, subjective preferences determine play and are then selected by material payoff over a slower evolutionary process. Here, holding the slow environment \(E\) and the closure law \(\ell\) constant during fast adjustment, subjective preferences first close under a fast law; material selection then operates on the closure laws, institutions, or transition rules that remain heterogeneous after closure.

Running example. A social-feedback closure law can be subcritical or supercritical. Below the critical feedback threshold, exposure moves preferences continuously and reversibly. Above it, temporary exposure can select a persistent preference branch. This example will return after the general results as the paper's main worked closure law.

For a fixed slow environment \(E\), Equation (1) summarizes the order of operations. Read it from left to right: choose a closure law \(\ell\) and a closure experiment \((q,\zeta)\); let preferences settle to \(C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)\); form the reduced subjective game \(\Gamma_{\ell,q,\zeta}^\star(E)\); compute its Nash equilibria; then evaluate the selected outcome by material value. In the unique-closure case, the dependence on \(q\) and \(\zeta\) is suppressed and the familiar notation \(\theta_\ell^\star(E)\), \(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E)\), and \(G_\ell(E)\) is used.

Order Of Analysis \[ (\ell,q,\zeta) \longmapsto C_\ell(E,q,\zeta) \longmapsto \Gamma_{\ell,q,\zeta}^\star(E) \longmapsto NE\!\left(\Gamma_{\ell,q,\zeta}^\star(E)\right) \longmapsto G_{\ell,q,\zeta}(E). \]

With Equation (1) in mind, the result can be stated without suspense. Nash equilibrium remains the solution concept. What changes is the payoff table to which Nash equilibrium is applied. Material selection remains a method of comparison. What changes is the object being compared: once initial preference differences have been erased by fast closure, the remaining post-closure selection problem is over laws, institutions, or environments that still produce different games.

Material payoff The external evaluator remains well defined. It need not be what players maximize.

Assumptions versus conclusions. The paper assumes a fast preference subsystem, a stated closure law, and separate subjective and material payoff objects. It does not assume that fast preference change is harmful. The conclusions are conditional: fast closure determines which subjective game is played; Nash invariance requires preservation of best responses; selection over initial tastes becomes weak when closure maps them to the same post-closure state; and social-feedback laws can be reversible or history-dependent depending on criticality.

The formal results follow this order. Lemma 1 gives the fast-closure reduction: after the short fast-adjustment period, the analyst solves the induced subjective game. Proposition 1 gives a sufficient condition under which the reduced subjective game and the material benchmark game have the same Nash set: every player has the same optimal responses under both payoff tables, for every possible mixed behavior of the other players. Proposition 2 shows how strict subjective and material maximizers can diverge after closure, and Example 1 gives a two-action construction in which the Nash set itself changes.

Theorem 1 introduces a closure-equivalence class, meaning a group of initial preference states that close to the same final utility representation. It says that a material selection rule observing only post-closure play cannot distinguish states inside such a class. Selection then moves to what still differs after closure: closure laws, institutions, or environments. Proposition 3 applies the same logic to platform rule choice: material loss follows from proxy/material misalignment on the feasible rule set, not from preference endogeneity alone.

The worked social-feedback section then specializes \(\ell\). It shows that a familiar logit/threshold closure law has a criticality boundary. Below the boundary, fast preference change is reversible comparative statics. Above it, temporary fashion or meme exposure can create persistent preferences and can lower a stated material evaluator.

The paper therefore separates three steps that are often collapsed: subjective equilibrium, material evaluation, and material selection. Fast preference closure changes the game sent into the equilibrium step; it does not by itself determine the material or welfare conclusion.

Recommender systems and AI-mediated environments are useful applications because they can choose exposure rules that affect future utility representations. They are not the paper's formal object. The formal claims are conditional comparative statics, not empirical claims about any particular platform or technology. The formal object is a finite normal-form game with a fast preference state, subjective payoffs, material payoffs, and selection over closure laws.

2. Related Work

Stable preferences and endogenous preferences. The stable-preference benchmark is associated with the Stigler-Becker view that economists can often treat tastes as fixed and explain behavior through prices, incomes, constraints, and household production. A large endogenous-preference literature relaxes that benchmark through habit, addiction, culture, institutions, identity, chosen preferences, and adaptive utility. This paper does not claim that endogenous preferences are new. It studies a specific timing limit: preferences close quickly before the game is solved.

Indirect evolutionary preferences and two-speed dynamics. The paper is closest to indirect evolutionary preferences and two-speed dynamics. In that literature, subjective preferences determine behavior, while material payoffs determine which preferences survive. The present paper keeps the same subjective/material distinction but interposes a fast preference-closure stage before play and material selection. The limiting exercise is narrow: given a fast preference subsystem and a finite game, characterize the reduced game, the conditions for Nash-set invariance, and the objects on which selection can act after closure.

Social feedback, cascades, and influence. The social-feedback mechanism deliberately borrows established mathematics from bandwagon demand, conformity, threshold contagion, social interactions, information cascades, and influence diffusion. The contribution is not the claim that fashion or herding is new. The new object is their role as a map from a pre-choice environment to the utility representation used in a later game. A meme, influencer, or recommender shock enters as an input to the closure law; the resulting fixed point or selected branch becomes the utility representation used in that game.

Recommender systems and proxy objectives. The platform application connects to work on recommender systems, engagement objectives, and feedback loops. The paper does not estimate any real platform effect and does not assume that platform objectives are harmful. It asks which formal condition prevents harm: if the proxy used to choose closure laws is aligned with the material evaluator, proxy choice is materially harmless on the feasible set; if proxy and material rankings reverse on some pair of rules, there is a two-rule feasible set on which proxy choice selects a materially inferior closure law.

3. Model

The model has four moving parts. First, there is a finite game: each player has a finite set of actions. Second, there is a fast preference state that determines the utility representation used for choice. Third, there is subjective utility, which players maximize. Fourth, there is material payoff, which is used for evaluation or selection. The point of the notation is to keep these objects separate.

Let \(N=\{1,\ldots,n\}\) be a finite set of players, \(A_i\) player \(i\)'s finite action set, and \(A=\prod_i A_i\) the set of pure action profiles. A pure action profile is \(a=(a_i)_{i\in N}\in A\). A mixed strategy is a probability distribution over actions: \(x_i\in\Delta(A_i)\). A mixed profile \(x\in X=\prod_i\Delta(A_i)\) records all players' mixed strategies. The slow environment is \(E\in\mathcal E\). The closure law is \(\ell\in\mathcal L\).

Player \(i\)'s subjective utility is \(U_i(a;\theta_i,E,\ell)\), where \(\theta_i\in\Theta_i\) is player \(i\)'s preference state. Player \(i\)'s material payoff is \(\pi_i(a;E,\ell)\). Equation (2) displays the two payoff objects side by side. The material payoff need not equal the subjective utility representation. The notation allows \(\ell\) to enter material payoff directly, for example if an institution changes costs or information as well as preferences; in applications where the law affects material outcomes only through behavior, the direct \(\ell\)-argument can be suppressed.

Payoff Objects \[ U_i(a;\theta_i,E,\ell) \quad\text{and}\quad \pi_i(a;E,\ell). \]

The preference state follows a fast dynamic. The function \(H\) is the fast adjustment rule, or vector field. The optional input \(\zeta\) records fast-clock exposure or transition inputs, such as a temporary social-media shock; if there is no such input, suppress \(\zeta\). A zero of \(H\) is a fixed point of the preference process. The dot in \(\dot\theta\) denotes a time derivative, and the parameter \(T_\theta\) is the preference-adjustment timescale. Sending \(T_\theta\to 0\) means that preferences settle before the slower variables move. With \(\theta=(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n)\), Equation (3) writes the fast law:

Fast Preference Dynamics \[ T_\theta \dot{\theta} = H(\theta;E,\ell,\zeta). \]

The maintained fast-adjustment assumption is fixed-state closure. During the fast adjustment, \(E\), \(\ell\), and the relevant input \(\zeta\) are held fixed or varied only as specified by the closure experiment. Let \(q\) denote the initial preference state at the start of the fast adjustment. When the fast limit exists, the closure map \(C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)\) records the selected post-adjustment preference state. The common unique-closure case is the special case in which the closure map is independent of \(q\) and \(\zeta\); then it can be written as the branch \(\theta_\ell^\star(E)\). Equation (4) records both objects.

Closure Map And Unique Branch \[ \begin{aligned} C_\ell(E,q,\zeta) &= \lim_{s\to\infty}\theta(s;q,E,\ell,\zeta),\\ \theta_\ell^\star(E) &= C_\ell(E,q,\zeta) \quad \text{for all relevant }(q,\zeta) \text{ under unique closure.} \end{aligned} \]

The formal results in Sections 4 through 7 are stated in the unique-closure notation because that is the clean benchmark. The branch-selected notation matters when the fast process has multiple stable fixed points, as in the social-feedback model in Section 8. A moving-slow-state model would require additional tracking assumptions; Appendix A.1 records that possible extension, but no result in the paper depends on it.

Unique Closure Fixed Point \[ H(\theta_\ell^\star(E);E,\ell,\zeta)=0, \qquad \theta(t)\to\theta_\ell^\star(E) \ \text{on the fast clock.} \]

The notation \(\theta_{i,\ell}^\star(E)\) means player \(i\)'s component of the unique settled preference vector \(\theta_\ell^\star(E)\). More generally, \(C_{i,\ell}(E,q,\zeta)\) is player \(i\)'s component of the branch-selected closure map. The reduced subjective game is the ordinary finite game obtained after replacing the pre-adjustment preference state by the post-closure state. Equation (6) defines the branch-selected version and then the unique-closure abbreviation used in the main theorems:

Reduced Subjective Game \[ \begin{aligned} \Gamma_{\ell,q,\zeta}^\star(E) &= \Bigl(N,\{A_i\}_{i\in N}, \{\,U_i(\cdot;C_{i,\ell}(E,q,\zeta),E,\ell)\,\}_{i\in N}\Bigr),\\ \Gamma_\ell^\star(E) &= \Bigl(N,\{A_i\}_{i\in N}, \{\,U_i(\cdot;\theta_{i,\ell}^\star(E),E,\ell)\,\}_{i\in N}\Bigr). \end{aligned} \]

Equation (7) defines the material benchmark game. It has the same players and actions as Equation (6), but it uses material payoffs instead of subjective utility:

Material Benchmark Game \[ \Gamma_\ell^\pi(E) = \Bigl(N,\{A_i\}_{i\in N}, \{\,\pi_i(\cdot;E,\ell)\,\}_{i\in N}\Bigr). \]

For any game \(\Gamma\), write \(NE(\Gamma)\) for its Nash set, the set of self-consistent mixed-strategy profiles. Some results compare sets of equilibria and do not need a single selected outcome. When a scalar material comparison is needed, fix an equilibrium-selection rule \(\sigma_\ell(E)\in NE(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E))\) and a material evaluator \(M:X\times\mathcal E\times\mathcal L\to\mathbb R\). Equation (8) defines the scalar material value:

Scalar Material Evaluation \[ G_\ell(E) = M\!\left(\sigma_\ell(E),E,\ell\right). \]

Set-valued equilibrium statements below do not depend on \(\sigma\). Scalar material comparisons do.

4. Fast Closure And Reduced Games

This section performs the first reduction. If the preference state reaches a unique attracting branch before play is analyzed, the analyst can replace the pre-adjustment preference state with the post-closure utility representation and solve the resulting subjective game.

Lemma 1. Fast Closure With The Slow Environment Held Constant

Fix \(E\), \(\ell\), and the relevant input \(\zeta\). In the unique-closure benchmark, suppose \(T_\theta\dot{\theta}=H(\theta;E,\ell,\zeta)\) has a unique globally attracting fixed point \(\theta_\ell^\star(E)\) on the relevant input class. Then, after the fast preference adjustment and in the limit \(T_\theta\to 0\), equilibrium analysis at \((E,\ell)\) is represented by the reduced subjective game \(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E)\). More generally, if the fast limit from initial state \(q\) exists and equals \(C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)\), then equilibrium analysis for that closure experiment is represented by \(\Gamma_{\ell,q,\zeta}^\star(E)\). The preference state is not an independent slow primitive at that point; it is the induced closure object.

Proof. See Appendix A.2. The point is that the fast-adjustment limit replaces the pre-adjustment preference state by the attracting branch, or selected branch, before equilibrium is computed.

What this says. Once fast adjustment is complete, the analyst can stop tracking the initial preference state and solve the game generated by the attracting post-closure state. The lemma does not say whether that game is good or bad; it says which game is being played.

5. Nash Invariance And Non-Invariance

Nash equilibrium is not a welfare claim. It is a consistency condition on players' choices: each player is optimizing against the others. In this paper, Nash equilibrium is computed in the reduced subjective game because subjective utility is what players maximize. The open question is whether that subjective equilibrium prediction agrees with the material-payoff benchmark. A selection-robust sufficient condition is that fast closure leaves every player's mixed best responses unchanged.

Let \(X_{-i}=\prod_{j\ne i}\Delta(A_j)\). For \(x=(x_i,x_{-i})\), write \(x(a)=\prod_j x_j(a_j)\). Expected subjective and material payoffs are:

Expected Payoffs \[ \begin{aligned} \bar U_{i,\ell}(x_i,x_{-i};E) &= \sum_{a\in A}x(a)\, U_i(a;\theta_{i,\ell}^\star(E),E,\ell),\\ \bar\pi_{i,\ell}(x_i,x_{-i};E) &= \sum_{a\in A}x(a)\, \pi_i(a;E,\ell). \end{aligned} \]

A best response is any strategy that maximizes payoff against the opponents' mixed profile. It is called a correspondence rather than a function because ties can produce several best responses. The corresponding mixed best-response correspondences are:

Best Responses \[ \begin{aligned} BR_{i,\ell}^\star(x_{-i};E) &= \arg\max_{x_i\in\Delta(A_i)} \bar U_{i,\ell}(x_i,x_{-i};E),\\ BR_{i,\ell}^\pi(x_{-i};E) &= \arg\max_{x_i\in\Delta(A_i)} \bar\pi_{i,\ell}(x_i,x_{-i};E). \end{aligned} \]
Proposition 1. Mixed Best-Response Invariance

If \(BR_{i,\ell}^\star(x_{-i};E)=BR_{i,\ell}^\pi(x_{-i};E)\) for every player \(i\) and every mixed opponent profile \(x_{-i}\in X_{-i}\), then \(NE(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E))=NE(\Gamma_\ell^\pi(E))\). For one fixed game, the condition is sufficient but not necessary.

Proof. See Appendix A.3. Equality of all mixed best-response correspondences gives equality of their fixed-point sets.

Plain implication. If fast closure only relabels utility without changing what any player wants to do against any mixed opponent profile, then the subjective and material games have the same Nash equilibria. This is the clean case in which endogenous utility does not change strategic prediction.
Corollary 1. Strategically Irrelevant Closure

Suppose that for every player \(i\) there exist \(\beta_i>0\) and \(\alpha_i(a_{-i};E,\ell)\), independent of \(a_i\), such that:

Opponent-Only Transformation \[ U_i(a_i,a_{-i};\theta_{i,\ell}^\star(E),E,\ell) = \alpha_i(a_{-i};E,\ell) + \beta_i\,\pi_i(a_i,a_{-i};E,\ell). \]

Then \(NE(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E))=NE(\Gamma_\ell^\pi(E))\).

Proof. For fixed \(x_{-i}\), the expected \(\alpha_i\) term is constant in \(x_i\). Multiplication by \(\beta_i>0\) preserves the argmax over \(x_i\). Hence subjective and material best-response correspondences coincide for every \(x_{-i}\), and Proposition 1 applies.

Proposition 2. Best-Response Non-Invariance Test

Suppose that for some player \(i\), actions \(a_i,b_i\in A_i\), and opponent mixed profile \(x_{-i}\), action \(a_i\) is the unique material best response and action \(b_i\ne a_i\) is the unique subjective best response:

Strict Maximizer Reversal \[ \begin{aligned} \{a_i\} &= BR_{i,\ell}^\pi(x_{-i};E),\\ \{b_i\} &= BR_{i,\ell}^\star(x_{-i};E). \end{aligned} \]

Then \(BR_{i,\ell}^\star(x_{-i};E)\ne BR_{i,\ell}^\pi(x_{-i};E)\). Hence the mixed best-response correspondence invariance condition in Proposition 1 fails. Nash sets may still coincide in special games; the proposition identifies failure of the strong invariance test, not a necessary-and-sufficient Nash-set separation. In a two-action choice set this condition is equivalent to a strict subjective/material ranking reversal between the two actions.

Proof. See Appendix A.5. The two correspondences are distinct singleton sets at the same mixed opponent profile.

Plain implication. If closure makes a player strictly prefer a different action than material payoff would recommend at the same opponent profile, the strong invariance test fails. The proposition does not claim every such failure changes the Nash set; Example 1 shows a minimal case where it does.
Example 1. A Two-Action Nash-Set Change

Let two players each choose \(A\) or \(B\). For both players and every opponent action, let material payoff satisfy:

Material Dominance \[ \pi_i(A,a_{-i};E,\ell)=1, \qquad \pi_i(B,a_{-i};E,\ell)=0 . \]

The material benchmark game has the unique Nash equilibrium \((A,A)\). Suppose the closure-law family contains a law \(\ell\) whose attracting branch induces the subjective representation:

Post-Closure Subjective Dominance \[ U_i(A,a_{-i};\theta_{i,\ell}^\star(E),E,\ell)=0, \qquad U_i(B,a_{-i};\theta_{i,\ell}^\star(E),E,\ell)=1 . \]

Then \(B\) is strictly dominant in the reduced subjective game, so \(NE(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E))=\{(B,B)\}\), while \(NE(\Gamma_\ell^\pi(E))=\{(A,A)\}\). The example is deliberately minimal: no equilibrium-selection issue is needed for fast closure to change the Nash set.

6. Selection After Closure

Selection means a rule that compares outcomes by their material consequences and favors some types, laws, or institutions over others. In the usual indirect-evolutionary picture, material payoff can select among preference types. The fast limit changes that target. If fast closure sends different initial preference states to the same post-closure utility representation, those initial differences no longer show up in play. A selection rule that observes only the reduced game and its material consequences has nothing left to select among inside that collapsed class.

Fix the relevant closure input \(\zeta\). Let \(Q_\ell\) be a set of initial preference states associated with closure law \(\ell\), and let \(C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)\) denote the post-closure preference state reached from initial state \(q\in Q_\ell\). Under unique closure, \(C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)=\theta_\ell^\star(E)\) for all \(q\in Q_\ell\) and all relevant \(\zeta\).

For an initial state \(q\), write \(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E;q,\zeta)\) for the reduced subjective game obtained from the post-closure state \(C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)\). Under common unique closure, \(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E;q,\zeta)=\Gamma_\ell^\star(E)\) for every \(q\in Q_\ell\). Define an equivalence relation \(q\sim_{E,\ell,\zeta} q'\) if \(C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)=C_\ell(E,q',\zeta)\). Initial states in the same equivalence class induce the same reduced subjective game.

A material selection rule is post-closure admissible if it depends on the initial state \(q\) only through the reduced game, selected post-closure play, and the material evaluator. Equivalently, it can be written as \(S(q;E,\ell,\zeta)=\Phi(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E;q,\zeta),E,\ell)\), or, after specifying an equilibrium-selection rule, as \(S(q;E,\ell,\zeta)=M(\sigma(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E;q,\zeta)),E,\ell)\). Direct observability or direct reward of the pre-closure state \(q\) is outside this theorem. This restriction is important: the theorem is about selection that sees the post-closure economy, not selection that separately rewards the historical origin of preferences.

Theorem 1. Selection Target Shift

Fix \(E\), \(\ell\), and \(\zeta\). If \(q\sim_{E,\ell,\zeta} q'\), then the initial states \(q\) and \(q'\) induce the same reduced subjective game and the same Nash set. Consequently, every post-closure admissible material selection rule is constant on each closure-equivalence class. Under common unique closure, all \(q\in Q_\ell\) lie in one equivalence class, so selection over initial preference states is degenerate in the \(T_\theta\to 0\) limit.

Proof. See Appendix A.6. The closure map identifies which initial states remain distinguishable after the fast adjustment.

What this says. Selection has not vanished. It has lost leverage over initial preference states that fast closure makes observationally identical to the post-closure selection rule. The surviving selection problem is over whatever still differs after closure, such as closure laws, institutions, or transition rules.

To compare closure laws, specify a finite active set \(\mathcal L_0\subseteq\mathcal L\), an equilibrium-selection rule, a material evaluator \(G_\ell(E)=M(\sigma_\ell(E),E,\ell)\), and a population dynamic or institutional choice rule. For example, if \(n_\ell\ge 0\) is the share of law \(\ell\), with \(\sum_{\ell\in\mathcal L_0} n_\ell=1\), a standard material selection equation is:

Selection Over Closure Laws \[ \dot n_\ell = n_\ell\left(G_\ell(E)-\bar G(E)\right), \qquad \bar G(E)=\sum_{k\in\mathcal L_0} n_kG_k(E). \]

The scalar equation is not the definition of Theorem 1. It is one possible post-closure selection equation obtained after the law-level objects have been specified.

7. Platform Proxy Choice

A platform application gives closure laws an institutional interpretation. A platform rule can be read as an exposure rule, ranking rule, recommendation rule, or interface rule that helps determine future preferences. Let a platform choose a rule \(r\in\mathcal R\). Each rule induces a closure law, a reduced subjective game, and a selected mixed equilibrium. The platform evaluates rules using a proxy \(V(r)\), such as engagement or retention. The analyst evaluates material consequences using \(G(r)\), such as welfare, health, survival, or another explicitly stated criterion.

Platform Rule As Closure Law \[ r \longmapsto \theta_r^\star(E) \longmapsto \sigma_r(E)\in NE\!\left(\Gamma_r^\star(E)\right) \longmapsto \bigl(V(r),G(r)\bigr). \]

The platform chooses \(r^V\in\arg\max_{r\in\mathcal R}V(r)\). A material planner would choose \(r^G\in\arg\max_{r\in\mathcal R}G(r)\).

Proposition 3. Proxy Alignment

For a fixed feasible set \(\mathcal R\), if \(\arg\max_{r\in\mathcal R}V(r)\subseteq\arg\max_{r\in\mathcal R}G(r)\), then every proxy-maximizing platform rule attains the feasible maximum of the material evaluator. A sufficient condition is that \(V\) and \(G\) have the same maximizer set on \(\mathcal R\); strict ordinal equivalence is sufficient when it preserves the relevant maximizers. Conversely, if there exist two rules \(r_1,r_2\) with \(V(r_1)>V(r_2)\) and \(G(r_1)<G(r_2)\), then on the feasible set \(\{r_1,r_2\}\), proxy maximization selects a materially inferior closure law.

Proof. See Appendix A.7. The first part is a fixed-feasible-set alignment result; the second is a two-rule reversal construction.

Plain implication. A platform can change preferences and behavior without lowering the stated material evaluator if its proxy selects material maximizers. The danger begins when the proxy that chooses the preference-producing rule ranks feasible rules differently from the material criterion.

The result is exact and conditional. Preference endogeneity and equilibrium movement do not imply material loss. Loss arises when the proxy that selects closure laws is not aligned with the material evaluator on the feasible set.

8. Social Feedback As A Closure Law

The preceding sections treat the closure law \(\ell\) abstractly. This section gives one concrete family: preference closure driven by social exposure. The economic interpretation is simple. A person sees a fashion, meme, influencer, or repeated platform recommendation. That exposure changes the person's taste state. The new taste state also changes what others observe as popular, which feeds back into their taste states. The fixed point of that loop is the post-closure preference vector used in the reduced game.

There are \(n\) agents. Agent \(i\)'s settled taste intensity is \(m_i\in[-1,1]\). Positive \(m_i\) means attraction to a fashion-coded action, while negative \(m_i\) means rejection. The baseline field \(h_i\) is the non-fashion component of taste. The nonnegative attention matrix \(W\) records social exposure: \(W_{ij}\) is the weight agent \(i\) places on agent \(j\)'s taste. The parameter \(\eta\ge 0\) is social reinforcement, \(\beta>0\) is responsiveness, \(z\) is a temporary meme or platform field, and \(\phi_i\) is agent \(i\)'s exposure loading. In the notation of the general model, the closure input \(\zeta\) is the scalar field \(z\) or the temporary path of that field.

Table 3. Objects in the social-feedback closure.

Object Meaning
\(m_i\)Settled taste intensity of agent \(i\).
\(h_i\)Baseline taste field before the temporary meme or influencer shock.
\(W_{ij}\)Attention weight from agent \(i\) to agent \(j\).
\(\eta\)Strength of social reinforcement.
\(\beta\)Responsiveness of tastes to the total field.
\(z\)Temporary fashion, meme, or platform exposure field.
\(\phi_i\)Loading of agent \(i\) on that exposure field.
\(T_m\)Fast timescale for social-feedback preference adjustment.
\(F_i\)Fashion-coded action in agent \(i\)'s action set.

The network fast dynamic and its fixed point are:

Network Social-Feedback Dynamics And Closure \[ \begin{aligned} T_m\dot m_i &= -m_i+ \tanh\!\left( \beta\left[ h_i+\eta\sum_j W_{ij}m_j+\phi_i z \right] \right),\\ m_i &= \tanh\!\left( \beta\left[ h_i+\eta\sum_j W_{ij}m_j+\phi_i z \right] \right). \end{aligned} \]

A temporary shock is modeled as part of the closure experiment: it is either held long enough for the fast subsystem to settle, or varied slowly relative to the fast clock. The fixed-point equation is deliberately borrowed from logit social interactions, smooth threshold models, and Ising-style mean-field models. The interpretation here is narrower than a general theory of fashion. The selected fixed point \(m=C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)\) is the preference state that closes before the strategic game is solved.

To embed this closure state in the finite game, let \(F_i\in A_i\) be agent \(i\)'s fashion-coded action and let \(\nu_i^0(a;E,\ell)\) be the baseline payoff representation before the social-feedback taste term. One simple embedding is:

Social Taste Embedding In Utility \[ \theta_i=m_i, \qquad U_i(a;m,E,\ell) = \nu_i^0(a;E,\ell) + \lambda_i m_i\,\mathbf 1\{a_i=F_i\}, \qquad \lambda_i>0. \]

Other embeddings are possible. This one is enough to show how a social-feedback closure state changes the payoff table of the later game: after \(m\) is selected, the reduced subjective game is obtained by substituting that \(m\) into \(U_i\). The propositions below are therefore conditional model results about this closure law, not empirical claims about every fashion cycle, social-media platform, or AI-mediated environment.

Proposition 4. Subcritical Social-Feedback Closure

If \(\beta\eta\rho(W)<1\), where \(\rho(W)\) is the spectral radius of \(W\), then the network social-feedback closure has a unique settled taste state. Small changes in \(z\) move that state continuously. When \(z\) is removed, the closure law itself has no hysteresis.

Subcritical Social-Feedback Condition \[ \beta\eta\rho(W)<1. \]

The product \(\beta\eta\rho(W)\) is the model's local feedback gain for taste reinforcement. It plays the role of a reproduction number: responsiveness times social reinforcement times network amplification. Below one, one round of imitation does not create enough new taste movement to sustain itself. Targeted exposure can still matter, but its effect is single-valued and reversible in this closure law.

Proposition 5. Near-Critical Influence Multiplier

At a stable fixed point \(m^\star\), let \(D=\operatorname{diag}(1-(m_i^\star)^2)\). If \(I-\beta\eta D W\) is invertible, the local response of settled preferences to a small exposure field is given by the social multiplier below. As \(\beta\eta\rho(DW)\) approaches one from below, the response can become large and concentrated along central attention directions to which \(D\phi\) has nonzero projection.

Local Influence Multiplier \[ \frac{\partial m^\star}{\partial z} = \beta\left(I-\beta\eta D W\right)^{-1}D\phi. \]

The matrix \(D\) measures local flexibility. Tastes near the extremes \(-1\) and \(1\) are less responsive; tastes near zero are more responsive. The inverse term is the amplification channel. This is the formal version of the claim that a targeted exposure field has the largest marginal effect when the audience is near a tipping surface.

The mean-field case is the cleanest microscope for persistent fashion. Let \(m\) be the average taste state, \(J\) the strength of average social reinforcement, and \(b=h+z\) the total field. The closure equation becomes:

Mean-Field Social-Feedback Closure \[ m=\tanh\!\left(\beta(Jm+b)\right). \]

If \(\beta J\le 1\), the mean-field closure has one stable state. If \(\beta J>1\), it has a hysteresis window. Define the spinodal taste level \(m_s\) and critical field \(B_c\) by:

Critical Exposure Field \[ m_s=\sqrt{1-\frac{1}{\beta J}}, \qquad B_c=Jm_s-\frac{\operatorname{arctanh}(m_s)}{\beta}. \]
Proposition 6. Hysteresis And Persistent Fashion

If \(\beta J>1\), then for every total field \(b\) with \(|b|<B_c\), the mean-field closure has two stable taste states separated by an unstable state. Starting on the low branch, a temporary positive shock that pushes \(b\) strictly above \(B_c\) eliminates that branch. If the shock is then removed and the baseline field lies inside \((-B_c,B_c)\), the path remains on the high branch.

Hysteresis means memory. Removing the shock need not undo its effect because the system has moved to a different stable preference branch. This is the point at which "exposure changed behavior" becomes a stronger claim: exposure changed the closure state from which later behavior is evaluated.

To compare the induced taste state with an outside yardstick, use the simple material evaluator \(G(m)=hm\). If \(h<0\), the evaluator favors the low-fashion state. This evaluator is intentionally minimal. It is not a welfare theorem; it is a diagnostic showing that a persistent taste branch can be bad by a stated material criterion even though it is stable under the induced preference process.

Persistent-Loss Window \[ |h|<B_c \quad\text{and}\quad B_c-h< z_{\max}. \]
Proposition 7. Temporary Exposure, Persistent Material Loss

If the persistent-loss window holds, \(h<0\), and the initial condition is on the low stable branch, then any temporary positive exposure shock \(z\in(B_c-h,z_{\max})\) can move the system to the high stable branch. After the shock disappears, the new taste state persists along that path and lowers \(G\).

9.2 Finite-Game Strategic Audit

The finite-game simulation has three messages. Neutral closure behaves as the theory predicts: it preserves best responses and selected outcomes. Random strategic closure usually changes the game. In this audit, proxy losses are small when the proxy is approximately aligned with material payoff and large when the proxy is independent or reversed.

The random finite-game audit is a stress test of the invariance condition, not evidence needed for the theorems. The audit generates 5,000 random two-player, two-action games per route. A neutral route applies strategically irrelevant preference movement. A strategic route adds random payoff distortions. Three proxy routes choose closure laws from an intensity grid using noisy material-aligned, independent, or misaligned proxy objectives.

Simulation design. The replication entry point is python3 scripts/run_model_selection_audit.py, with seed 20260620. Material payoff entries are drawn independently from the uniform distribution on \([-1,1]\). For routes with multiple equilibria, the selected pure equilibrium maximizes the relevant ranking payoff; if no pure equilibrium is available, the script uses the interior mixed equilibrium when it lies in the unit square and otherwise falls back to the uniform distribution. An outcome shift is counted when the distance between the selected material and subjective outcome distributions exceeds \(0.25\). For a reported rate \(p\) with \(N=5{,}000\), the Monte Carlo standard error is \(\sqrt{p(1-p)/N}\), which is at most \(0.0071\).

These numbers are not estimates of any real platform; they show how restrictive the exact invariance conditions are in random finite games. 10. Welfare And Interpretation

In ordinary revealed-preference welfare analysis, the choices or preferences observed at the end of the process often carry normative weight. That move becomes fragile when the process being evaluated helps produce those final preferences. Final-preference welfare is then not a stable welfare criterion by itself. The analyst must say what is being evaluated: allocations, material payoff, terminal preferences, the transition path, or the rule that changes preferences.

The model separates three questions that are often compressed. First, which utility representation is used for play? In the fast limit, it is the representation induced by closure. Second, are equilibrium predictions materially invariant? They are guaranteed to be invariant when fast closure preserves mixed best-response correspondences. Third, is the induced transition materially or normatively acceptable? That depends on the material evaluator, meta-preferences, or admissible transition laws.

The social-feedback mechanism makes the third question concrete. Below criticality, exposure shifts preferences in the ordinary comparative-static sense. Above criticality, the closure law is history-dependent: a temporary exposure shock can determine which stable preference branch later behavior uses. In that region, evaluating only the final induced preference can miss the role of the transition rule that produced it.

Final-preference welfare is not invariant when the process being evaluated also produces the final preferences. An allocation path can be attractive according to the induced terminal utility representation and unattractive according to initial preferences, material payoff, or a meta-preference over how preferences are changed. The paper does not select a welfare criterion. It shows why a criterion must specify whether it evaluates allocations, terminal preferences, transition laws, or paths.

Empirically, the framework points to two measurements: the speed of preference or exposure adjustment relative to the relevant slow variables, and the alignment between the proxy that induces closure and the material evaluator. A broader working-paper version retains a macro timescale diagnostic for this purpose. The present submission keeps that diagnostic outside the main argument.

11. Conclusion

The limiting result is simple. If preferences adapt first, the game is no longer built from primitive utility. It is built from utility produced by the preference-formation process.

In finite games, the formal object after fast adjustment is a reduced subjective game. Nash equilibrium is computed there because that is the game players are actually optimizing. Material payoff is evaluated separately. Mixed best-response preservation is a strong sufficient condition under which Nash predictions agree with the material-payoff benchmark after fast closure. Under common unique closure, material selection rules that observe only post-closure play cannot distinguish initial preference states that induce the same reduced game. Selection then moves to the laws, institutions, or transition rules that produce different reduced games.

The social-feedback example shows one way the closure law can be generated. If reinforcement is weak, induced preferences move continuously and reversibly. If reinforcement crosses a critical threshold, a temporary fashion, meme, influencer, or platform exposure can move the economy to a different persistent preference branch. That branch can then feed into the same Nash and selection machinery as any other closure law.

The platform example gives the result a concrete institutional interpretation. A rule that shapes future utility is not automatically harmful, and a changed equilibrium is not automatically a loss. The material claim requires a misalignment statement: the proxy choosing the closure law must select rules that the material evaluator ranks below feasible alternatives. That is the paper's main discipline. Fast endogenous utility changes where the economic analysis must look; it does not replace the need for equilibrium, selection, or welfare reasoning.

Appendix A. Proof Details

A.1 Remark On Moving Slow States

The main text holds \(E\) and \(\ell\) constant during the fast preference adjustment and treats \(\zeta\) as part of the specified closure experiment. In singular perturbation language, this is a fixed-slow-state boundary-layer closure: a short initial fast period is analyzed while the slow variables do not move. A moving-slow-state extension would require additional uniform attraction and regularity assumptions: for example, compact active sets, local Lipschitz continuity of \(H\) in \(\theta\), a unique uniformly attracting branch \(\theta_\ell^\star(E)\), a Lipschitz slow path \(E(t)\), and fixed \(\ell\) between discrete institutional revisions. Under such assumptions, standard singular perturbation arguments would support tracking of \(\theta_\ell^\star(E(t))\) after the initial fast-adjustment period on finite slow-time intervals away from discontinuities of \(\ell\). The paper does not use this as a theorem; the formal results require only fixed-state closure.

A.2 Proof Of Lemma 1

Fix \(E\), \(\ell\), and \(\zeta\). On the fast time \(s=t/T_\theta\), the preference subsystem is \(d\theta/ds=H(\theta;E,\ell,\zeta)\). Under unique closure, every initial state in the relevant basin converges to \(\theta_\ell^\star(E)\) as \(s\to\infty\). Thus, along any sequence \(T_\theta\downarrow 0\) observed after the short fast-adjustment period and before \(E\) or \(\ell\) changes, the payoff table used for play converges pointwise to \(U_i(\cdot;\theta_{i,\ell}^\star(E),E,\ell)\) for each player \(i\). Because action sets are finite, pointwise convergence of the payoff table is enough to define the limiting reduced finite game \(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E)\). If convergence from initial state \(q\) instead selects \(C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)\), the same pointwise argument gives the branch-selected game \(\Gamma_{\ell,q,\zeta}^\star(E)\). Nash equilibrium analysis after fast adjustment is therefore Nash equilibrium analysis in the relevant reduced game.

A.3 Proof Of Proposition 1

For every finite game, a mixed Nash equilibrium is a profile \(x\in X\) such that \(x_i\in BR_i(x_{-i})\) for every player \(i\). If \(BR_{i,\ell}^\star(\cdot;E)=BR_{i,\ell}^\pi(\cdot;E)\) for all \(i\), then the set of profiles satisfying the subjective fixed-point condition is exactly the set satisfying the material fixed-point condition. Hence the Nash sets coincide. The condition is not necessary because equality of fixed-point sets does not require equality of correspondences at profiles that are not fixed points.

A.4 Proof Of Corollary 1

For fixed \(x_{-i}\), expected subjective payoff equals the expected opponent-only term plus \(\beta_i\) times expected material payoff. The opponent-only term does not vary with \(x_i\), and \(\beta_i>0\) preserves the ordering of all \(x_i\). Therefore the subjective and material argmax sets coincide at every \(x_{-i}\). Proposition 1 gives equality of mixed Nash sets.

A.5 Proof Of Proposition 2

At the same opponent mixed profile, \(BR_{i,\ell}^\pi(x_{-i};E)=\{a_i\}\) and \(BR_{i,\ell}^\star(x_{-i};E)=\{b_i\}\), with \(a_i\ne b_i\). The two singleton correspondences are therefore not equal. Thus mixed best-response invariance fails. In a two-action choice set, a strict subjective/material ranking reversal between the two actions is exactly this strict-maximizer reversal.

A.6 Proof Of Theorem 1

If \(q\sim_{E,\ell,\zeta} q'\), the closure map gives \(C_\ell(E,q,\zeta)=C_\ell(E,q',\zeta)\). Thus every player's subjective payoff table is the same under the two initial states, so \(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E;q,\zeta)=\Gamma_\ell^\star(E;q',\zeta)\) and their Nash sets coincide. For any post-closure admissible selection rule \(S(q;E,\ell,\zeta)=\Phi(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E;q,\zeta),E,\ell)\), the argument of \(\Phi\) is identical for \(q\) and \(q'\), so \(S(q;E,\ell,\zeta)=S(q';E,\ell,\zeta)\). The same conclusion holds for the equilibrium-selection form \(S(q;E,\ell,\zeta)=M(\sigma(\Gamma_\ell^\star(E;q,\zeta)),E,\ell)\). In the unique-closure case, every initial state in \(Q_\ell\) maps to \(\theta_\ell^\star(E)\), so \(Q_\ell\) is one equivalence class and initial preference-state selection is degenerate. A model that directly observes or rewards the pre-closure state \(q\) violates post-closure admissibility and is not covered by the theorem.

A.7 Proof Of Proposition 3

Fix \(\mathcal R\). If \(\arg\max_{\mathcal R} V\subseteq\arg\max_{\mathcal R} G\), then every proxy maximizer \(r^V\) is also a material maximizer, so \(G(r^V)=\max_{r\in\mathcal R}G(r)\). Equality of maximizer sets is a direct sufficient condition. Strict ordinal equivalence is also sufficient when it preserves the relevant maximizers and ties on \(\mathcal R\). For the reversal construction, suppose there exist \(r_1,r_2\) with \(V(r_1)>V(r_2)\) and \(G(r_1)<G(r_2)\). On the feasible set \(\{r_1,r_2\}\), proxy choice selects \(r_1\), while material choice selects \(r_2\). The proxy-maximizing rule is therefore materially inferior on that feasible set.

Appendix B. Social-Feedback Proof Details

B.1 Proof Of Proposition 4

Let \(F(m)=\tanh(\beta[h+\eta Wm+\phi z])\), where the hyperbolic tangent is applied componentwise. For any two taste vectors \(m,m'\), the derivative of \(\tanh\) is bounded above by one in absolute value, so the sup-norm Lipschitz modulus of \(F\) is bounded by \(\beta\eta\|W\|\) for any compatible matrix norm. More sharply, for a nonnegative matrix \(W\), the spectral-radius condition \(\beta\eta\rho(W)<1\) gives a weighted sup norm in which \(F\) is a contraction. Banach's fixed-point theorem gives a unique fixed point, and the same contraction gives continuous dependence on the field \(z\). With a unique branch, removing \(z\) returns the closure law to the unique no-shock fixed point; hysteresis requires multiple stable branches, which the contraction region excludes.

B.2 Proof Of Proposition 5

At a stable fixed point, write the fixed-point equation as \(m=F(m,z)\). Differentiating with respect to the scalar exposure field gives \(\partial m/\partial z=\beta D(\eta W\,\partial m/\partial z+\phi)\), with \(D=\operatorname{diag}(1-(m_i^\star)^2)\). Rearranging gives \((I-\beta\eta DW)\partial m/\partial z=\beta D\phi\). If the matrix is invertible, the derivative is the stated multiplier. When the largest relevant eigenvalue of \(\beta\eta DW\) approaches one from below, the inverse becomes large along the corresponding eigendirection. The exposure vector must load on that direction through \(D\phi\); otherwise that near-critical direction is not excited by the shock.

B.3 Proof Of Proposition 6

For the mean-field equation \(m=\tanh(\beta(Jm+b))\), stable and unstable folds occur where the fixed-point curve is tangent to the forty-five-degree line. Differentiating the right side with respect to \(m\) gives \(\beta J(1-m^2)\). A fold therefore solves \(\beta J(1-m_s^2)=1\), so \(m_s=\sqrt{1-1/(\beta J)}\) when \(\beta J>1\). Substituting the fold condition into \(m=\tanh(\beta(Jm+b))\) gives \(b=\operatorname{arctanh}(m)/\beta-Jm\). The positive critical magnitude is \(B_c=Jm_s-\operatorname{arctanh}(m_s)/\beta\). For total fields inside \((-B_c,B_c)\), the curve has two stable fixed points separated by an unstable one. A shock that pushes \(b\) beyond \(B_c\) removes the low branch; after the shock is removed, the high branch remains stable inside the hysteresis window.

B.4 Proof Of Proposition 7

The first inequality \(|h|<B_c\) places the no-shock baseline inside the hysteresis window, so both high and low stable branches exist at baseline. The second inequality \(B_c-h<z_{\max}\) allows a shock \(z\in(B_c-h,z_{\max})\), so the total field \(h+z\) crosses strictly above \(B_c\), eliminating the low branch and moving the path to the high branch. When \(h<0\), the material evaluator \(G(m)=hm\) ranks lower \(m\) above higher \(m\). Since the shock path ends on the high branch after removal, the final state has lower \(G\) than the original low-branch state.
