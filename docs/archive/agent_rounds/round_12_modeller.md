# Round 12 Modeller Memo

## Verdict

The Round 11 style revision did not damage the theorem spine. The GEB draft now says the main result plainly, and the theorem statements still have the correct scope: fixed \(E\), fixed \(\ell\), unique closure, finite games, subjective utility distinct from material payoff, and post-closure admissible selection rules.

The next risk is not rigor. It is repetition and label choice. Some phrases still sound like internal scaffolding: "operator order," "operators that are often collapsed," and "robust/strong invariance test." These are not wrong, but they slow the reader when the concept is simple: preferences settle, players optimize, material consequences are evaluated.

## Mathematical Guardrails

Keep Proposition 1 exactly as a sufficient mixed best-response condition. Do not imply it is necessary for Nash-set equality in a single game.

Keep Proposition 2 as best-response non-invariance plus a separate example of Nash-set change. The friendly prose should never collapse those two claims.

Keep Theorem 1 tied to post-closure admissibility. The result is not that no one can ever observe initial preferences; it is that selection rules that evaluate only the reduced game and post-closure consequences cannot distinguish states within a closure-equivalence class.

Keep Proposition 3 as an alignment theorem. Preference-producing platforms are not mechanically harmful; misalignment on the feasible rule set is the object that generates material loss.

## Recommended Edits

1. Rename the GEB display label "Operator Order" to "Order Of Analysis" or "Timing Of Analysis." The display itself can stay.

2. Replace the sentence "The paper therefore separates three operators..." with "The paper therefore separates three steps..." The math behind it remains the same; the prose becomes less high-formal.

3. In the introduction table, make the selection row explicitly conditional on the selection rule observing only post-closure play. This protects Theorem 1 from over-reading.

4. In Section 8, replace "behaves like the theorem says it should" with a more journal-style sentence such as "behaves as the theory predicts."

5. In the working paper, replace "invariance discipline" in the Proposition 1 proof with "sufficient condition." Also replace "most unsettling implication" with a less rhetorical phrase.

## Next Round Focus

After these edits, the article should undergo a full-reader pass: does each section begin by telling the reader why it exists? The remaining challenge is not notation but narrative hierarchy.
