When Preferences Move Faster Than Equilibrium

Endogenous utility, material-capacity feedback, and Nash equilibrium

Working paper draft. Prepared for comment.

JEL codes: C72, D01, D11, D83, D91, I12. Keywords: endogenous preferences, utility, Nash equilibrium, material capacity, social media, AI, addiction, loneliness, competition.

Abstract

Modern systems can change what feels rewarding before real capacities can catch up. A feed, market app, peer group, institution, or AI product may make one action feel best today. That action can then change what is possible tomorrow: friendship, solvency, health, learning, trust, family agency, or institutional competence.

The model gives one disciplined map of this feedback loop. In the simplest case, one stock matters. Fast preference change has three endings. If repair is strong enough, capacity recovers. If damage and repair balance badly, the system has two resting places: a low-capacity trap and a high-capacity state. A threshold separates them. If damage is stronger still, the high-capacity state disappears. In all three cases, people may still choose rationally given the payoff they face. The problem is not irrationality. The problem is the feedback loop that forms the payoff.

A warning is not always a cure. A signal that only says "capacity is falling right now" may slow the slide. In the one-capacity model, it may leave the bad resting place intact. At rest, nothing is falling, so the signal goes quiet. Removing the bad state needs a persistent force: capacity itself must change what feels rewarding, or competition must reward the rule that preserves capacity. If competition rewards engagement instead, the capacity-draining rule can spread while friendship, solvency, health, or trust falls.

This gives a test, not a one-cause theory of modern life. For AI companions, sports betting, hyper-palatable food, appetite-changing medicines, dating retreat, appearance markets, and political outrage, ask four questions. What forms the payoff at choice time? What substitute is chosen? What capacity changes? What score decides which rule survives?

1. The Puzzle In Plain Words

People choose according to what feels worth doing now. Modern systems can shape that feeling. A person can choose well by today's payoff and still have less capacity tomorrow.

An isolated adult may prefer an AI companion tonight because it is responsive, low-risk, and always available. A bettor may want the next in-game wager because the app makes risk feel like part of the match. A teenager may avoid courtship because digital substitutes feel safer. A citizen may prefer outrage media because a familiar voice feels more vivid than slow verification. These choices need not be mistakes at the moment they are made. The environment has helped shape what feels worth choosing.

Subjective payoffWhat feels worth doing at choice time.
SubstituteThe quick reward or relief that may replace practice.
CapacityThe slower stock that keeps other choices possible.
Outcome metricThe named stock the analysis cares about.

For a lonely user, the substitute may be the companion. The capacity may be the ability to handle ordinary contact. The score may reward retention, or it may reward restored social life. Those are different economies.

The payoff is not fake. If it guides choice, it is real for choice. The question is different: does the system that forms the payoff build the capacity we care about, or deplete it? And does competition select the systems that build that capacity?

Results

  1. Bridge. The loop rebuilds capacity.
  2. Trap. Low capacity makes the substitute attractive, and the substitute keeps capacity low.
  3. Collapse. The high-capacity state disappears under the current environment.
  4. Alarm. In the one-capacity logit model, a current-decline warning does not move the model's long-run resting places.
  5. Selection. Competition rewards the score it is given. It does not automatically reward survival capacity.

2. The Model In Words

The model has two clocks. The fast clock forms the payoff used for choice. A feed changes salience. A platform changes friction. A peer group changes status.

The slow clock moves capacity. Social skill, solvency, metabolic health, trust, and institutional competence do not always move at the same speed as the feed. No clock is assumed. It must be measured. A platform rule may be fast. A social skill may be slow. An institution may be either.

ExposureA feed, product, peer norm, interface, institution, or AI system changes what feels rewarding.
ChoiceAgents optimize inside the induced payoff; in strategic settings this is the Nash step.
CapacityThe behavior changes a slower stock such as solvency, health, social skill, learning, or trust.
SelectionRules expand or shrink according to the competitive score that the environment rewards.

Capacity makes the outside option usable. If social skill is high, human contact is less costly. If solvency is high, risky credit is less tempting. If trust is high, verification is easier. Substitute use can erode that capacity. Repair may fail near the bottom because the capacity needed for repair is already missing.

That is where the threshold comes from. In the AI companion case, the question is blunt: does synthetic interaction build enough social capacity to make human contact easier later, or does it replace the practice needed for that contact?

Competition enters only after the score is named. A rule may win because it preserves capacity. It may win because it captures attention. It may win because it earns revenue, reproduces biologically, or survives inside an institution. These scores can agree. They can also conflict. The model separates relative victory from material survival.

3. What The Model Says

Bridge

Fast preference formation is not automatically harmful. In the one-capacity model, the system reaches a high-capacity state when the substitute is mild, repair is strong, or capacity stays high enough to protect the outside option. An AI companion is a bridge if it lowers anxiety and helps the person return to human contact with more capacity.

Trap

Low capacity can make the substitute attractive. Heavy substitute use can keep capacity from rebuilding. High capacity can also persist because the outside option is still usable. Between the two lies a threshold. In the two-basin case, the path rises above it and falls below it.

Collapse

The upper state can disappear. Then the system is not merely trapped below a threshold. It has lost the high-capacity destination under the current environment. To recover, the forces pushing capacity up or down must change. In a betting case, this is the point where solvency is so damaged that ordinary budgeting no longer restores the previous option set.

What changes the prediction. A change matters in the model only if it changes the path: it lowers damage, raises repair, moves the state across the threshold, removes the low trap, or changes what competition rewards.

Does Reality Correct The System Automatically?

Not automatically. The model separates two signals. One signal says, "this is getting worse right now." In the one-capacity diagnostic, that signal can slow the path, but it may not change where the system settles. At rest, it goes quiet.

A capacity signal is different. It says that the stock itself changes what feels rewarding. In the test case, that channel removes the low-capacity trap because it still acts when the system is not moving. A person may feel bad about retreating, but if retreat has become stable, the bad feeling need not rebuild the social skill they lost.

Competition Is Corrective Only Under The Right Metric

Competition is Darwinian. That does not make it benevolent. Competition selects the rule with the higher score. If the score is material survival, the bridge wins in the diagnostic. If the score is engagement, the sink wins in the diagnostic. The same selection process gives opposite outcomes under different scoreboards.

4. Nash Equilibrium And Competition

Nash does one job here. After the rule forms payoffs, Nash asks whether each player is choosing coherently given those payoffs and the others' choices. The equilibrium can be coherent while capacity falls.

Competition decides which payoff-forming systems spread. But first we must say what competition rewards. If the score rewards capacity, rules that score poorly on capacity lose share. If the score rewards engagement, attention, status, or short-run revenue, the damaging rule may spread. "Competition will fix it" is not a theorem. The claim needs the score.

Selection claim. Relative share depends on the score. Absolute survival depends on the growth of the population, capital, or institution carrying the rule. If every available rule depletes the relevant capacity, the end point can be disappearance, not a fitter taste.

This disciplines the Darwinian language. "Fitness" is not a mood. It must be named: reproduction, solvency, health, learning, institutional continuity, verification capacity, or another material metric. Different metrics can disagree. That disagreement is part of the economics.

Political outrage makes the point concrete. If restraint loses attention, escalation can be a best response for each broadcaster, politician, or influencer. Nash explains why escalation can be stable after payoffs are formed. Competition explains why the attention-scoring environment may spread while verification, trust, and correction acceptance decay.

5. A Full Example: AI Companions And Loneliness

The cleanest case is AI companionship. The WHO Commission on Social Connection and the US Surgeon General treat loneliness as a health and community problem. De Freitas et al., Common Sense Media, and Malfacini document the emerging AI-companion setting.

Suppose a companion is always available, responsive, nonjudgmental, and tuned by a platform. Exposure is the companion's availability and emotional fluency. The subjective payoff is the value of synthetic interaction compared with costly human contact. The substitute is time spent in the synthetic relationship instead of attempts at ordinary social contact.

The material capacity is not "happiness" in general. It is something measurable: offline contact attempts, friendship repair, invitations accepted, social anxiety after use, time in mixed social settings, or durable human ties. A bridge lowers the cost of contact and preserves those stocks. A sink gives enough synthetic reward that the outside stock decays. The same user can choose rationally under either design. The difference is the path of measured social capacity.

The threshold result says that small changes may have different effects depending on the starting stock. Above the threshold, synthetic interaction can be a scaffold that reduces anxiety and returns the user to human contact with higher capacity. Below the threshold, the same local reward can stabilize retreat if outside contact becomes too costly relative to the induced payoff. The model does not say loneliness vanishes. It says an alarm based only on "am I getting worse right now?" can go quiet once a low-contact routine becomes stable.

Competition is the Darwinian part. Imagine two companion systems. A health insurer, school, employer, clinical trial, or public agency rewards the bridge when offline contact or social anxiety improves. A platform ranking system rewards the sink when retention, session length, paid attachment, or daily dependence rises. Material-viability competition expands the bridge if it builds long-run capacity. Engagement competition expands the sink if it captures more time, even when human social capacity falls. AI companionship is not intrinsically good or bad in this model. The score decides which design gains share.

The test is concrete. Randomize or instrument exposure to companion designs. Measure loneliness now and offline contact capacity later. Then observe which score governs product survival. A fixed-preference account is enough if exposure changes only convenience or information. The feedback account is needed if exposure changes the payoff of human contact and behavior then changes later social capacity.

6. Candidate Domains

The same mechanism can be tested elsewhere. The table is a research menu, not proof that one model explains everything. Each row is a falsifiable map.

Sports betting shows the competition point. Handle, retention, and in-game engagement can rise while household solvency falls. A fixed-preference story can describe gambling demand. The feedback story asks whether the interface changes the payoff of risk and whether repeated play changes future solvency.

GLP-1 medicines show the positive case. A fast change in appetite payoff can be a bridge if it rebuilds durable metabolic capacity. It is a sink only if it creates dependence without rebuilding the stock that makes healthier eating and weight maintenance feasible outside the product.

Political outrage shows strategic stability. Escalation can be individually rational after the attention payoff is formed. It can also degrade verification capacity. A fixed-preference account can describe demand for partisan media. The feedback account asks whether the media environment changes the payoff of outrage and whether repeated exposure lowers later correction acceptance.

Dating retreat is the threshold case. Avoidance may feel safer when courtship skill is already low. But avoidance can also keep that skill from rebuilding. Appearance markets give another test. Filters or procedures may be bridges if they restore agency. They are sinks if they make yesterday's baseline feel unacceptable and raise the next round of spending.

Each row must name a substitute, a measurable capacity, and a competitive score before it becomes evidence.

7. Empirical Strategy

The model becomes empirical when the capacity is named and measured. For loneliness, measure friendship, social anxiety, and offline practice. For betting, measure savings, credit scores, missed payments, and debt service. For food, measure metabolic markers and appetite regulation after exposure changes. For politics, measure trust, correction acceptance, and cross-cutting exposure.

The test has three steps. Does exposure change what feels rewarding? Does the changed payoff alter behavior? Does the behavior change later capacity? Without all three links, the case is weaker than the model.

The panel test must separate feedback from stable differences across people or places. The researcher needs three links: exposure and capacity predicting later payoff, behavior and payoff predicting later capacity, and payoff predicting substitute use. Appendix D writes these equations.

A fixed-preference account is enough when exposure changes convenience, information, or prices but not what feels rewarding. It is also enough when behavior does not feed back into future capacity. A feedback account is needed when exposure changes payoff, payoff changes behavior, and behavior changes the future capacity that shapes later payoff.

Identification requires care. State-space choice models are useful when preference parameters are latent. Random-intercept cross-lagged panel models help separate within-person movement from stable between-person differences. Randomized deactivation and feed experiments, such as Allcott et al., Braghieri, Levy, and Makarin, and Guess et al., are especially valuable because they shift exposure before all material stocks have had time to adjust.

8. Relation To Existing Economics

Stigler and Becker move apparent taste changes into shadow prices and consumption capital. Grossman models health as a durable capital stock. Becker and Murphy model addiction through intertemporal complementarity. Bowles, Bisin and Verdier, and Bernheim et al. put preference formation inside institutions, transmission, or chosen worldviews.

Ely and Yilankaya, Dekel, Ely, and Yilankaya, and Sandholm separate subjective preferences, equilibrium play, and evolutionary selection. Koszegi and Rabin and Genicot and Ray make reference points and aspirations endogenous. Brock and Durlauf and Arthur show how social interactions and increasing returns create multiple states. Recommender-system work such as Jiang et al. and Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan supplies the modern preference-forming environment.

The contribution is the order of events. Payoffs form quickly. Choice follows, with Nash equilibrium in strategic cases. Behavior then changes slower capacity. Finally, payoff-forming rules compete under an explicit score. This order can describe both harm and repair. It does not call preferences fake. It does not assume competition improves welfare.

9. What The Result Distinguishes

The model does not tell people what to want. It asks what changes the path. A change can lower inducement, lower sensitivity, reduce damage, strengthen repair, make capacity reduce the substitute's pull, move the state across a threshold, or change the competitive score. These are model comparisons, not moral categories.

Technology is not classified by its surface form. An AI companion, recommender, medicine, school, workplace, or political institution is a bridge when it leads back into durable capacity. It is a sink when it wins the local payoff game while reducing the capacity that would make alternatives feasible later. Competition can reward either form.

The model also cuts against weak optimism. Current-decline warnings below a threshold need not move the state in the one-capacity model. The low-capacity equilibrium can absorb the warning into the same payoff that created the trap. Correction requires a changed force, a threshold crossing, or competition that moves mass toward a more viable rule.

10. Conclusion

Utility can be endogenous without being unreal. People optimize the payoff they have. Modern systems can change that payoff quickly. Material capacity often moves more slowly. Then it feeds back into what will feel rewarding next.

The model gives three endings: repair, trap, and collapse. A warning alone may not remove the bad state. A real capacity channel can. Competition can help too, but only when the score rewards capacity rather than engagement.

Nash tells us when choices fit the payoffs people face. Selection tells us which payoff-forming systems spread. The missing object is the loop between fast payoffs and slow capacity. That loop can build capacity. It can also drain it.

Appendix A. General Formal Model

A.1 Primitives

There is a finite set of players \(I=\{1,\ldots,n\}\). Player \(i\) has action set \(A_i\), and \(A=\prod_i A_i\). The slow state is \(K\in\mathcal K\). In applications \(K\) may be scalar or vector-valued. The slow environment is \(E\). The preference-forming rule is \(\ell\). Exposure or input is \(z\). Player \(i\)'s preference state is \(\theta_i\), and subjective utility is \(U_i(a;\theta_i,K,E,\ell)\). The material evaluator is \(G(K,a,E,\ell)\), or player-specific material payoff \(\pi_i(a,K,E,\ell)\) when needed. The material law of motion is \(F(K,x,E,\ell,z)\); it describes how equilibrium behavior changes the slow capacity state. The evaluator \(G\) ranks consequences, while \(F\) moves the capacity stock.

Fast preference state \[ \varepsilon\dot\theta_i=H_i(\theta_i;K,E,\ell,z),\qquad 0<\varepsilon\ll 1 \]

If the fast dynamic has a selected attracting state, write it as \(C_i(K,E,\ell,z)\). This is the settled preference state used for choice while \(K\), \(E\), and \(\ell\) are held fixed on the fast clock.

Settled subjective payoff \[ U_i^\star(a;K,E,\ell,z)=U_i(a;C_i(K,E,\ell,z),K,E,\ell) \]

The induced game \(\Gamma^\star(K,E,\ell,z)\) has the original action sets and the settled subjective payoffs \(U_i^\star\). A mixed-strategy profile \(x\) is a Nash equilibrium of that induced game when every player's mixed strategy maximizes expected settled subjective payoff against the others.

Nash step after payoff formation \[ x^\star\in NE\bigl(\Gamma^\star(K,E,\ell,z)\bigr) \]

The slow state then changes according to a material law. The displayed theorem uses a selected continuous equilibrium branch. If the induced game has several equilibria and no branch is selected, the right-hand side should be treated as a correspondence or differential inclusion with additional assumptions; the paper does not need that generality for the scalar applications.

Reduced slow dynamic \[ \dot K\in\left\{F(K,x,E,\ell,z):x\in NE\bigl(\Gamma^\star(K,E,\ell,z)\bigr)\right\} \]
Fast-limit reduction

Assume that for each fixed \((K,E,\ell,z)\) in the region studied, the fast preference dynamic has a selected normally hyperbolic attracting branch \(C(K,E,\ell,z)\), with attraction uniform on the compact region considered, and that initial preference states lie in the basin of that branch. Assume no branch switching or bifurcation occurs on the interval studied. Assume also that the induced game has a selected Nash equilibrium branch \(x^\star(K,E,\ell,z)\) that is continuous on the region considered, and that the slow vector field \(F(K,x,E,\ell,z)\) is regular. Then along that interval, the \(\varepsilon\to0\) limit is governed by \(\dot K=F(K,x^\star(K,E,\ell,z),E,\ell,z)\).

Proof. Under the stated normal-hyperbolicity and uniform-attraction assumptions, the fast state approaches the selected branch while \(K\), \(E\), \(\ell\), and \(z\) are fixed on the fast clock; this is the standard singular-perturbation logic associated with Fenichel. Substituting that branch into subjective utility gives the induced game. Continuity of the selected equilibrium branch and regularity of \(F\) give the reduced slow vector field after substitution.

A.2 Local Feedback Multiplier

When \(K\) is scalar and the equilibrium branch is locally single-valued and differentiable, write the reduced law as \(\dot K=\Phi(K)\). The derivative of \(\Phi\) is the local feedback multiplier. For vector-valued \(K\), the same expression is a Jacobian and local stability depends on eigenvalues.

Local feedback multiplier \[ D\Phi(K)=F_K+F_x\bigl(x_\theta C_K+x_K\bigr) \]

The term \(C_K\) is the effect of capacity on the settled preference state. The term \(x_\theta\) is the effect of that preference state on behavior. The term \(F_x\) is the effect of behavior on the material capacity. Their product is the preference-capacity feedback loop. Negative feedback is locally self-correcting; sufficiently positive feedback can produce thresholds or instability.

A.3 Nash Invariance

Let \(BR_i^\star(x_{-i};K,E,\ell,z)\) be player \(i\)'s best-response correspondence in the induced subjective game, and let \(BR_i^\pi(x_{-i};K,E,\ell)\) be the best-response correspondence under the material payoff benchmark.

Best-response preservation

If \(BR_i^\star(x_{-i};K,E,\ell,z)=BR_i^\pi(x_{-i};K,E,\ell)\) for every player and every opponent mixed profile, then the induced subjective game and the material benchmark game have the same mixed Nash equilibrium set at that state. The condition is sufficient; special games may have the same equilibrium set even when the full best-response correspondences differ.

Proof. A mixed Nash equilibrium is a fixed point of the product of best-response correspondences. Equality of the correspondences at every opponent profile gives equality of the fixed-point sets.

A.4 Competition And Selection Over Rules

Let \(s_\ell\) be the share of aggregate mass \(N\) governed by preference-forming rule \(\ell\), with \(s_\ell\ge0\) and \(\sum_\ell s_\ell=1\). Let \(S_\ell\) be the score that determines relative competition among rules, and let \(g_\ell\) be the material growth, reproduction, persistence, or institutional growth rate of the mass governed by \(\ell\). Let \(\omega\ge0\) be the intensity of competition across rules, and let \(N\) be total population mass, capital mass, or institutional scale. The convention used here separates relative prevalence from aggregate material scale: \(S\) governs shares, while \(g\) governs \(N\).

Selection over preference-forming rules \[ \dot s_\ell=\omega s_\ell\left(S_\ell-\sum_r s_rS_r\right), \qquad \dot N=N\sum_r s_rg_r \]

This equation is not an additional welfare assumption. It is a way to state the Darwinian question after the competitive score and material growth rate have been named. Relative competition can select the rule with the highest \(S_\ell\), while absolute survival depends on the average \(\sum_r s_rg_r\). If all available rules imply negative material growth, the limiting result may be disappearance of the population, institution, or practice rather than survival of a better preference type.

Competition selects the metric, not objective welfare by definition

Consider two rules, \(A\) and \(B\), with competitive scores \(S_A\) and \(S_B\). If \(\omega>0\) and \(S_A>S_B\) on an interval, then any interior share \(s_A(0)\in(0,1)\) rises monotonically on that interval; if the score ordering persists indefinitely, \(s_A\) converges to one. If \(S_A>S_B\) but \(g_A

Proof. In the two-rule case, \(\dot s_A=\omega s_A(1-s_A)(S_A-S_B)\). The sign is positive for interior \(s_A\) exactly when \(S_A>S_B\), yielding monotone convergence to one. The absolute mass equation uses \(g\), not \(S\); if the relative competitive score differs from material growth, relative victory need not imply positive material growth.

Appendix B. Scalar Capacity Model

The scalar model used for the numerical diagnostics is the special case in which the fast subsystem directly yields a substitute share \(p(K,z)\) and the slow law is one-dimensional. The form is deliberately austere. It is the smallest normal form that allows self-correction, a threshold trap, and collapse-prone capacity loss.

Scalar substitute share \[ p(K,z)=\sigma\{\beta(q+z-\rho K)\},\qquad \sigma(y)=\frac{1}{1+\exp(-y)} \]
Scalar capacity law \[ \Phi(K)=\alpha+rK^2(1-K)-dK-Lp(K,z) \]

The feasible dynamic is the projected dynamic on \([0,1]\): in the interior, \(\dot K=\Phi(K)\); at \(K=0\), outward negative drift is held at the lower boundary; at \(K=1\), outward positive drift is held at the upper boundary.

Projected scalar dynamic \[ \dot K= \begin{cases} \max\{0,\Phi(0)\}, & K=0,\\ \Phi(K), & 0

The derivative is:

Scalar local multiplier \[ \Phi'(K)=r(2K-3K^2)-d+L\beta\rho p(K,z)\bigl[1-p(K,z)\bigr] \]

The final term is the induced-preference feedback. It is largest when the substitute share is near one half and small when the substitute is almost never or almost always chosen. This explains why the middle region can be unstable even when the low and high states are stable.

To test whether reality corrects the loop automatically, add a current-drift signal to the fast subjective payoff. Let \(\chi\ge0\) measure how salient current material improvement or deterioration is:

Current-drift reality signal \[ p_\chi(K)=\sigma\{\beta[q+z-\rho K+\chi\Phi_\chi(K)]\}, \qquad \Phi_\chi(K)=\alpha+rK^2(1-K)-dK-Lp_\chi(K) \]
Current deterioration does not move interior steady states

For the scalar logit model above, take \(\chi,L,\beta\ge0\). Then for each interior \(K\), the implicit equation for \(p_\chi(K)\) has a unique solution, and adding the current-drift signal \(\chi\Phi_\chi(K)\) leaves the set of interior steady-state capacities unchanged. It can change local slopes and adjustment speeds, but it cannot remove the low-capacity steady state by itself.

Proof. For fixed \(K\), the left side minus the right side of the implicit share equation is \(p-\sigma\{\beta[q+z-\rho K+\chi(R(K)-Lp)]\}\), where \(R(K)=\alpha+rK^2(1-K)-dK\). Its derivative with respect to \(p\) is \(1+\chi L\beta\sigma(1-\sigma)>0\), so the solution is unique. If \(K^\star\) is an interior steady state under the signal model, then \(\Phi_\chi(K^\star)=0\), so the signal term in the logit field is zero at \(K^\star\). The fixed-point equation for \(p_\chi(K^\star)\) is therefore the original logit equation. Hence \(K^\star\) is a root of the original scalar drift. The reverse implication is identical: at an original interior root the drift signal is zero, so the signal-augmented and original logit equations coincide.

Two-basin capacity trap

Suppose the projected scalar dynamic has exactly three interior equilibria \(K_L<K_U<K_H\) in the relevant interval, no attracting boundary state in that interval, and \(\Phi'(K_L)<0\), \(\Phi'(K_U)>0\), and \(\Phi'(K_H)<0\). Then \(K_L\) and \(K_H\) are locally stable, \(K_U\) is locally unstable, and the interval is split into two local basins separated by \(K_U\).

Proof. In one dimension, a fixed point with negative derivative of the vector field is locally attracting and a fixed point with positive derivative is locally repelling. With exactly the three stated interior equilibria and no attracting boundary state in the relevant interval, continuity implies the sign of \(\Phi\) alternates across the simple roots. Initial conditions on one side of the unstable root move toward the low stable root; initial conditions on the other side move toward the high stable root.

Conditional derivatives and existence of traps

For interior \(K\), the direct derivatives are \(\partial\Phi/\partial L=-p\), \(\partial\Phi/\partial z=-L\beta p(1-p)\), \(\partial\Phi/\partial \rho=L\beta Kp(1-p)\), and \(\partial\Phi/\partial\beta=-L(q+z-\rho K)p(1-p)\). Thus damage and exposure lower the drift pointwise, the capacity-protection channel raises the drift at fixed \(K>0\), and sensitivity has a sign that depends on whether the substitute field \(q+z-\rho K\) is positive or negative. Parameter changes can create or remove low-high traps by changing the root pattern of \(\Phi\); the capacity-trap calibration gives one such constructive case.

Proof. The derivative expressions follow by differentiating the logit formula and the scalar drift. The existence claim follows constructively from the reported calibration, where the computed roots satisfy the two-basin conditions in the preceding proposition. More general monotone comparative statics are not asserted because \(\beta\) and \(\rho\) affect both levels and slopes of the vector field.

The baseline capacity-trap calibration used for Table 1 and Figures 1-2 sets \(\alpha=0.235\), \(r=2.90\), \(d=0.66\), \(L=0.198\), \(\beta=21.0\), \(q=0.51\), and \(\rho=0.855\). The self-correcting scenario lowers \(L\) and \(\beta\). The repair scenario raises \(\alpha\). The current-drift and competition audits are generated by `scripts/run_self_correction_analysis.py`; the material-feedback baseline is generated by `scripts/run_material_feedback_analysis.py`.

Appendix D. Empirical Specification

Let \(\theta_{it}\) be a measured or latent subjective payoff state, \(K_{it}\) a material capacity, \(z_{it}\) exposure, \(x_{it}\) observed substitute behavior, and \(W_{it}\) prices, income, constraints, and other controls. A minimal panel representation is:

Empirical preference-formation equation \[ \theta_{i,t+1}=\alpha_i+\lambda_t+\varphi\theta_{it}+\gamma z_{it}-\rho K_{it}+u_{i,t+1} \]
Empirical capacity-feedback equation \[ K_{i,t+1}=\eta_i+\tau_t+\psi K_{it}+b x_{it}+c\theta_{it}+v_{i,t+1} \]
Empirical behavior equation \[ x_{it}=m(\theta_{it},K_{it},z_{it},W_{it})+\varepsilon_{it} \]

The fixed-preference account is adequate when the exposure and capacity feedback terms are small or disappear after controls and stable heterogeneity are included. The feedback account is stronger when exposure predicts the payoff state, the payoff state predicts behavior, and behavior predicts the future capacity that then predicts later payoff.
