# Governance Channel

This note records the current treatment of government and regulation in the
material-capacity feedback model.

## Core Interpretation

Government is not an exogenous rescue device. It is a slower collective
feedback channel: a society observes, argues over, and institutionalizes a
response to perceived material loss. The response may be accurate, late,
captured, symbolic, unenforced, or effective.

The parsimonious model adds one domain-specific governance instrument, `R`,
and one implementation-quality shifter, `Q`. The instrument responds to
measured collective loss and then changes the same levers already used in the
model:

- exposure or inducement `z`;
- damage from the substitute `L`;
- repair capacity `alpha` or `r`;
- the action set or enforcement constraint;
- the competitive score `S` that determines which rules expand.

`R` is the rule itself: tax, ban, product standard, enforcement intensity,
liability rule, ranking obligation, licensing rule, or public investment. `Q`
is the state's ability to make the rule real: administrative capacity, rule of
law, regulatory quality, corruption control, jurisdiction, review capacity, and
political aggregation. Unless a separate law of motion is added, `Q` is not a
dynamic state.

## Why This Fits

Regulation has historically acted on behaviors and preference-forming
environments without needing to erase preferences directly. Crime enforcement,
tobacco control, gambling limits, platform rules, food safety, and advertising
rules all operate by changing costs, exposure, product design, repair channels,
or market selection.

This fits the paper's logic. A rule succeeds only if it changes the capacity
path. A rule that merely reports harm is an alarm. A rule that changes exposure,
damage, repair, enforcement, or selection can be a path-changing force.

## Observable Government Variables

Empirical work should measure the rule and implementation quality separately.
Useful `Q` proxies include:

- World Bank Worldwide Governance Indicators: government effectiveness,
  regulatory quality, rule of law, and control of corruption;
- OECD regulatory-policy measures: stakeholder engagement, agile/flexible
  design, international impact assessment, risk-based enforcement, and policy
  review;
- domain-specific enforcement: inspections, penalties, compliance rates,
  black-market share, litigation delay, and jurisdictional coverage.

Useful `R` measures are domain-specific: tax rates, advertising bans, product
standards, licensing rules, detection intensity, penalties, reporting duties,
ranking obligations, self-exclusion rules, and public investment.

## Success Conditions

Government regulation is likely to change the model's outcome when:

- the relevant capacity loss is observable before the trap becomes deep;
- the regulatory rule changes the reduced capacity drift, not only the public
  alarm signal;
- enforcement capacity is high enough to prevent evasion or displacement;
- the rule changes the competitive score faced by firms, platforms, or local
  institutions;
- the response arrives faster than the harmful capacity erosion.

Failure modes are equally important:

- delay relative to fast payoff formation;
- capture or rent seeking;
- symbolic rules that leave `z`, `L`, repair, and `S` unchanged;
- black-market or offshore substitution;
- fragmented jurisdiction;
- measurement error that tracks outrage rather than material capacity.

The key empirical interaction is `R x Q`: a law on the books should move
exposure, damage, repair, enforcement, or selection more where implementation
quality is higher.

## Article Use

The article should discuss government as a factor in the dynamics, not as
advice. The question is not whether government should act. The question is what
kind of governance state is produced, which model lever it changes, and whether
the observed capacity path changes afterward.
