## What Improved

The abstract is much clearer. It now answers the reader's first question: "What is the result?" The three-paragraph structure works: limiting conclusion, formal condition, platform implication.

The introduction is substantially better. The "preferences settle, players optimize, analyst evaluates" paragraph gives a usable mental model before the operator display. The new table is helpful because it separates what survives from what changes. It should stay in the GEB draft unless later length constraints force compression.

The interpretation blocks after Lemma 1, Proposition 1, Proposition 2, Theorem 1, and Proposition 3 are valuable. They do not replace the theorem statements; they explain how to read them. This is exactly the right compromise between rigor and accessibility.

Sections 6, 8, and 9 now lead with conclusions. Section 6 no longer makes the reader infer the selection result from notation. Section 8 states the simulation message before the design details. Section 9 says the welfare problem plainly: final preferences are not a stable welfare criterion when the process being evaluated helps produce them.

The conclusion is stronger. It gives the paper's discipline in ordinary language: fast endogenous utility changes where analysis must look, but it does not license slogans about equilibrium, selection, or harm.

## Risks

There is some repetition across the abstract, introduction, takeaway boxes, and conclusion. Most of it is productive at this stage, but the final writer should remove duplicated phrases rather than duplicated ideas. In particular, "Nash equilibrium remains the method" and "selection shifts target" are repeated often enough that later versions should vary the language.

The introduction table is useful, but a GEB referee may see it as slightly working-paper-like. Keep it for now because it solves the user's actual readability problem. If the paper is later cut for journal submission, compress the table into a paragraph rather than deleting the content.

Some terms still sound heavier than necessary when they are doing expository work: "operator order," "robust invariance discipline," and "post-closure admissible." They are acceptable as formal labels, but surrounding prose should use ordinary language first.

The working paper now has a friendly abstract, but its introduction remains more expansive and motivational than the GEB version. That is fine for circulation, but final arXiv/public PDF generation should confirm the broader tone still feels professional.
