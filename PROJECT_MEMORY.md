## Current Thesis

Modern environments can change subjective payoffs quickly. Agents can then make
coherent choices under those induced payoffs. Those choices may change slower
material capacities, and future payoff formation depends on those capacities.
Selection then acts on preference-forming rules under whatever competitive
score is operative.

The result is not "preferences are fake" and not "Nash disappears." Preferences
are endogenous. Nash remains the strategic consistency condition after payoffs
are formed. Selection remains meaningful only after the competitive score and
material growth object are named.

## Model Objects

- `subjective payoff`: what the agent optimizes at choice time;
- `preference-forming rule`: environment or institution that forms subjective
  payoffs;
- `substitute behavior`: quick reward or relief that may crowd out capacity;
- `material capacity`: slow stock changed by behavior;
- `competitive score`: score that determines which rule spreads.

## Durable Lessons

- Endogenous preferences alone are not novel; the paper needs the feedback
  from induced payoff to material capacity and back.
- The "why now" claim is about time-scale acceleration and synchronized
  exposure: internet, social media, recommenders, and AI can move subjective
  payoffs before material capacity, institutions, markets, or selection catch
  up.
- Government is an endogenous governance channel, not an outside rescue. It
  matters when collective rules change exposure, damage, repair, enforcement,
  or the competitive score; it fails when delayed, captured, symbolic,
  unenforced, or displaced.
- A current-improvement/current-decline alarm is not a general
  self-correction mechanism in the minimal model.
- Competition can repair only when the score rewards capacity-preserving rules.
- Examples are useful only when they specify the measurable capacity and
  competitive score.
- The main text should be readable before the appendix is opened.
