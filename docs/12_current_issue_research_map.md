# Current Issue Research Map: Story-First Article Pivot

Date: June 24, 2026.

## Purpose

The combined article has pivoted from theorem-first exposition to a story-first
working paper with technical details in the appendices. The main text now uses
current economic, cultural, political, and international puzzles to motivate the
same mathematical object: fast preference closure can make locally rational
choices diverge from a slower evaluator such as health, fertility, solvency,
social capacity, learning, trust, or institutional continuity.

The four research agents were assigned distinct scans:

- Economic: fertility, housing, debt, betting, AI productivity, work, NEET,
  loneliness, GLP-1s, inflation sentiment.
- Cultural/social: loneliness, AI companions, dating and sex retreat,
  lookmaxxing, youth mental health, attention and education, radicalization,
  influencers, food, social withdrawal, religion/community sorting.
- Political: outrage feeds, algorithmic divisiveness, news influencers,
  institutional distrust, election legitimacy, AI deepfakes, political
  violence, democratic backsliding, immigration, climate identity, crime fear,
  vibecession, science/vaccine trust, youth disengagement.
- International/geopolitical: East Asian and European fertility, reproductive
  agency gaps, global loneliness, TikTok news, AI information operations,
  migration backlash, online nationalism, war identity, climate anxiety,
  AI labor, AI companions.

## Chosen Ten Main-Text Cases

1. Low fertility and the wanted-children gap.
   - Closure: status competition, intensive parenting, housing, career risk,
     partner-market friction, fear of the future.
   - Evaluator: population continuity or realized reproductive agency.
   - Sources: OECD Society at a Glance 2024; UNFPA State of World Population
     2025; Eurostat fertility statistics; CDC/NCHS births.
   - Caveat: fertility is not a moral duty; the evaluator must be explicit.

2. Housing and delayed adulthood.
   - Closure: the subjective threshold for "ready" adulthood moves through
     comparison over homes, neighborhoods, schools, and family platform quality.
   - Evaluator: household formation, fertility, financial resilience.
   - Sources: Harvard JCHS State of the Nation's Housing; NAR first-time-buyer
     profile.
   - Caveat: supply, rates, zoning, income, and insurance remain direct
     constraints. Preference closure is a multiplier, not a substitute.

3. Loneliness amid constant connection.
   - Closure: mediated contact and entertainment relieve social pain while
     reducing embodied social practice.
   - Evaluator: health, friendship, trust, resilience.
   - Sources: WHO Commission on Social Connection; US Surgeon General advisory.
   - Caveat: online communities can be protective, especially for isolated
     people.

4. Dating and sex retreat.
   - Closure: rejection avoidance, dating-app discouragement, pornography,
     gaming, safety fears, reputational risk, and economic insecurity raise the
     utility of non-courtship substitutes.
   - Evaluator: intimacy capacity, pair bonding, household formation, fertility.
   - Sources: Ueda et al. 2020 JAMA Network Open; CDC YRBS 2023 trend report.
   - Caveat: some retreat reflects autonomy, safety, and lower coercion.

5. Teen identity, attention, and appearance loops.
   - Closure: public self-narration, metrics, filters, comparison, and
     appearance optimization make temporary moods into rewarded identities.
   - Evaluator: mental health, learning, attention, identity flexibility.
   - Sources: Pew teen social media and technology 2024; Pew teens and mental
     health 2025; CDC YRBS; ASPS cosmetic statistics.
   - Caveat: online identity communities can be life-saving.

6. AI companions.
   - Closure: always-available empathy, memory, personalization, and low
     rejection make synthetic companionship attractive.
   - Evaluator: loneliness relief, social capacity, human relationships,
     agency.
   - Sources: Common Sense Media teen AI companions 2025; De Freitas et al.
     AI companions working paper; Malfacini AI & Society 2025.
   - Caveat: short-run loneliness reduction is valuable but not identical to
     long-run social capacity.

7. Sports betting, BNPL, and consumer debt.
   - Closure: frictionless risk and checkout convert fandom or identity
     consumption into repeated immediate reward.
   - Evaluator: solvency, savings, liquidity, household resilience.
   - Sources: NBER sports betting and household finances; Federal Reserve SHED
     report; American Gaming Association tracker.
   - Caveat: some legal betting and BNPL use may be entertainment or liquidity
     smoothing rather than pathology.

8. Ultra-processed food and GLP-1 preference repair.
   - Closure: food environments train craving; GLP-1s chemically alter appetite
     utility.
   - Evaluator: metabolic health, agency, longevity.
   - Sources: CDC/NCHS ultra-processed food data; GLP-1 consumer behavior and
     medical literature.
   - Caveat: income, access, biology, stress, side effects, and discontinuation
     matter.

9. Political outrage and news influencers.
   - Closure: parasocial trust, anger, enemy exposure, and format familiarity
     make institutional verification less rewarding.
   - Evaluator: truth-tracking, institutional trust, peaceful contestation.
   - Sources: Pew news influencers 2024; HKS Misinformation Review TikTok
     election engagement; PNAS Nexus divisive content ranking; Gallup/Pew
     institutional trust.
   - Caveat: influencers can democratize expertise and reach audiences
     institutions have lost.

10. Migration and international identity backlash.
    - Closure: border images, threat framing, housing/service pressure, crime
      narratives, and national identity make openness or threat feel locally
      natural.
    - Evaluator: integration, labor supply, rights, policy stability, alliance
      and global-public-good capacity.
    - Sources: OECD International Migration Outlook; UNHCR Global Trends; Pew
      US border attitudes; Gallup immigration concern.
    - Caveat: backlash cannot be reduced to preference manipulation; real state
      capacity, housing, services, and wage pressures matter.

## Article Implication

The core result now reads:

Fast preference closure makes utility a state variable produced by social and
technical systems. Nash equilibrium still describes strategic consistency after
that state is formed. Selection still asks what persists, but in the fast limit
the selected objects are increasingly closure laws and institutions rather than
initial tastes. A wedge appears only relative to a named evaluator. The same
preference-shaping technology can harm or repair depending on its proxy.

## Open Empirical Tests

- Speed: how quickly do stated values, attention, habits, and social norms move
  after exposure?
- Reversibility: do preferences return when exposure ends, or does a new branch
  persist?
- Proxy alignment: what objective selected the preference-shaping environment?
- Capacity: does the system build skill and opportunity outside itself, or
  increase dependency on itself?
- Selection level: do durable differences appear at the level of initial tastes
  or at the level of schools, platforms, AI designs, laws, and institutions?
