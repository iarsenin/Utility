# Round 6 GEB Modeller Memo

Role: Modeller. Personality: exacting scientist; game-theory first; skeptical of broad empirical or platform claims unless they are formally pinned down.

Task: review `paper/article_v1.html` and current results with a Games and Economic Behavior submission in mind. I did not edit article files or code.

## Bottom Line

The paper is moving in the right direction, but the GEB-facing version must become visibly and unapologetically a finite-game theory paper. The current draft still reads partly as a broad working paper on social media, AI, welfare, and empirical timescales. That is acceptable for a working-paper version. It is risky for GEB.

For GEB, the article should be organized around one central theorem package:

```text
fast preference closure -> reduced subjective game
reduced subjective game -> Nash-invariance / non-invariance
closure laws -> material selection target shift
platform proxy choice -> application of closure-law selection
```

The empirical WDI section should not be in the main body of a GEB submission. It is useful as a working-paper diagnostic and as a motivation for future empirical work, but it does not identify preference closure, platform manipulation, or material welfare. Keeping it in the main GEB version dilutes the theory contribution and invites avoidable referee objections.

## 1. What Must Change For GEB Fit

### Make the finite-game object primary

GEB readers should see immediately that the primitives are a finite normal-form game, subjective payoffs, material payoffs, a fast closure law, and a selection/evaluation operator. The current introduction reaches this eventually, but too much reader attention is spent on social media/AI before the formal object is fully established.

Recommended GEB framing:

1. Start with finite games in which the utility representation used for play is generated by a fast state variable.
2. Distinguish subjective utility from material payoff.
3. State the singular limit: the fast preference state closes before equilibrium is computed.
4. State the central formal implication: Nash equilibrium is computed in the induced subjective game, while material selection evaluates closure laws that survive the fast limit.
5. Use recommender systems and AI as applications, not as the paper's identity.

The title may remain `Fast Preference Closure`, but the subtitle should become more game-theoretic:

```text
Endogenous Utility, Nash Equilibrium, and Selection in Finite Games
```

or:

```text
Nash Equilibrium and Selection When Preferences Close Fast
```

### Promote the theorem sequence

The main formal results should be numbered and stated as a coherent theorem sequence:

- Lemma 1: Fast closure reduces the game to `Gamma_ell^*(E)`.
- Proposition 1: mixed best-response preservation implies Nash-set preservation.
- Corollary 1: positive affine transformations plus opponent-only terms preserve mixed Nash sets.
- Theorem 1: selection over initial preference states is degenerate under common unique closure; selection acts on closure laws.
- Proposition 2: proxy alignment is sufficient for no material loss under platform rule choice.

This sequence is good. The GEB version should make it more complete and less essay-like. The proof sketches need to become real proofs, either in the body or in a formal appendix.

### Make contribution relative to evolutionary preferences sharper

The closest GEB/JET-style neighborhood is indirect evolutionary preferences and two-speed dynamics. The current paper says the timing is reversed. That is the right wedge, but it needs a sharper mathematical sentence:

```text
In indirect evolutionary preference models, subjective preferences are selected by material payoff over a slower evolutionary process. Here, for each fixed slow state, subjective preferences are first selected/generated by a fast closure law; only the induced closure laws or institutions are then available for material selection.
```

This is the contribution. It is not "preferences are endogenous"; that is already old. It is not "platforms may manipulate preferences"; that is not the GEB theorem. It is the order of operators:

```text
standard slow preference selection: play -> material payoff -> preference-type selection
fast closure model: closure law -> induced preference state -> play -> material evaluation -> law selection
```

This operator-order comparison should appear in the introduction and related work.

## 2. Theorem And Proof Gaps Blocking GEB Submission

### Gap A: Lemma 1 is a reduction statement, not yet a singular perturbation theorem

Current Lemma 1 fixes `E` and `ell`. That is mathematically fine if the paper clearly presents it as a fixed-slow-state boundary-layer result. But a GEB referee may ask what happens when `E` evolves.

Required fix:

- Keep Lemma 1 as the finite-game boundary-layer lemma.
- Add a precise assumption block:

```text
For each compact subset of slow states and each active closure law, the fixed point theta_ell^*(E) is unique, globally attracting, and the attraction is uniform.
```

- Then either:
  - avoid claiming a moving-slow-state theorem, or
  - add a formal appendix proposition saying that, under standard uniform attraction and continuity assumptions, the reduced process tracks `theta_ell^*(E(t))` as `T_theta -> 0`.

The current draft says a full moving-slow-state theorem would require more assumptions. That is honest for a working paper. For a GEB submission, either include the theorem or explicitly define the contribution as a fixed-state comparative statics paper.

My recommendation: do not attempt a full Fenichel theorem in the main text. Add a short appendix proposition with finite-dimensional compact assumptions and cite singular perturbation theory. Keep the main body finite-game and clean.

### Gap B: Equilibrium selection is under-specified for material comparisons

The set-valued Nash results are fine. Scalar material claims require `sigma_ell(E)`. The paper names an equilibrium-selection map, but GEB referees will ask whether results depend on it.

Required fix:

- Separate set-valued statements from scalar evaluations everywhere.
- For Theorem 1, phrase the core result set-valuedly first:

```text
common closure implies the same reduced subjective game and the same Nash set.
```

Then add:

```text
for any fixed selection rule sigma, scalar material evaluations are also identical.
```

This protects the theorem from looking selection-rule dependent when the core claim is not.

### Gap C: The selection theorem is too verbal

Theorem 1 is directionally correct but too close to a proof sketch. It should explicitly define the selected class and the equivalence relation induced by closure.

Suggested formalization:

```text
Let q be an initial preference state and let C_ell(E,q) be the closure map. In the unique-closure case, C_ell(E,q)=theta_ell^*(E) for all q in Q_ell. Define q ~ q' iff C_ell(E,q)=C_ell(E,q'). Then all q in the same equivalence class induce the same reduced subjective game and the same Nash set. Therefore any material selection rule that depends on q only through post-closure play is constant on equivalence classes.
```

This is the clean mathematical statement. The current version says the same idea, but GEB wants the object on which selection becomes degenerate.

### Gap D: Best-response invariance condition is sufficient, but no generic non-invariance proposition exists

The simulations show that random strategic closure often changes mixed best responses. For GEB, a simple analytic non-invariance proposition would be stronger than a random-game frequency.

Add a proposition or observation:

```text
If closure changes player i's payoff difference between two actions at some opponent mixed profile from positive to negative, then the subjective and material best-response correspondences differ at that profile. Hence Nash predictions are not invariant in general.
```

This is nearly tautological, but useful. It gives the random-game audit a formal anchor: the simulation is not establishing the theorem; it is illustrating how often the formal condition fails under one stress distribution.

### Gap E: Proxy alignment proposition needs tie handling

Proposition 2 is correct in spirit. It should be made tie-robust.

Current statement:

```text
If argmax V subset argmax G, no material loss.
```

This is already the right tie-robust condition. The proof should specify:

```text
For every selected r^V in argmax V, G(r^V)=max_R G.
```

If the platform uses a tie-breaking rule, either assume it selects from `argmax V` arbitrarily and the inclusion covers all selections, or state the result for every proxy maximizer. Do not use language implying a unique maximizer unless uniqueness is assumed.

## 3. Empirical/WDI Content: Demote, Retain, Or Cut

### GEB submission version

Move the WDI section out of the main text. Options:

1. Put it in an online appendix as "Empirical discipline and calibration targets."
2. Cut it entirely from the initial GEB submission.
3. Retain only one short paragraph in the conclusion saying that the framework suggests measuring closure speeds and proxy/material alignment.

My recommendation: option 1 for the working-paper PDF, option 3 for the GEB manuscript.

Reason: the WDI exercise is careful, but it does not observe preference states, platforms, algorithms, or subjective utility. In a GEB submission, it will pull referee attention away from the finite-game contribution and toward identification. That is the wrong battlefield.

### Working-paper version

Keep WDI, but clearly label it:

```text
Empirical Appendix: Timescale Diagnostics
```

It should be used to show that timescales can be measured, not to support the platform-control mechanism. The current wording mostly does this. For the working paper, keeping the WDI section is useful because it shows the project is not only philosophical.

### Computational finite-game audits

Retain in both versions, but treat differently:

- Working paper: keep figures and tables in the body.
- GEB submission: one compact simulation section or appendix. The main body should present the audit as a robustness illustration after the propositions, not as evidence needed for the theorem.

For GEB, the finite-game audit should be described as:

```text
a computational stress test of the invariance conditions
```

not:

```text
empirical evidence
```

This distinction matters.

## 6. What Not To Overclaim

The following claims should not appear in the GEB version:

- "AI changes preferences in real time" as an asserted empirical fact.
- "Gen Z preferences have changed" as evidence.
- "Internet adoption causes welfare changes" based on WDI.
- "Fast endogenous preferences imply material harm."
- "Platforms manipulate utility" unless framed as an application with a specified closure law.
- "The framework is empirically validated."

The claims that can appear:

- If preferences close before play, the game solved by Nash equilibrium is the induced subjective game.
- Material Nash predictions are invariant only under best-response preservation.
- Under common unique closure, initial preference heterogeneity is erased before material selection.
- Selection can still operate on closure laws, institutions, or transition rules.
- Material loss under platform rule choice is conditional on proxy/material misalignment.
- Computational audits illustrate that the invariance condition is restrictive in random finite games.
